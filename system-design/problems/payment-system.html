Design: Payment System

Examples: Stripe, PayPal, Square, Adyen

Difficulty: Hard (Staff-Level) | Time: 45-60 minutes

Staff-Level Signals

  • Correctness over availability: Financial systems demand exactly-once semantics and strong consistency -- a stark contrast from typical "AP" system design answers.
  • Domain modeling: Double-entry bookkeeping, ledger immutability, and reconciliation are non-negotiable for real payment platforms.
  • Regulatory awareness: PCI DSS, SOX, PSD2/SCA -- demonstrating knowledge of compliance constraints shows senior judgment.
  • Failure reasoning: Staff engineers obsess over what happens when things go wrong -- double charges, partial failures, and network partitions.

1. Requirements Clarification

Functional Requirements ▼
  • Process payments: Accept one-time and recurring payments from buyers on behalf of merchants.
  • Multiple payment methods: Credit/debit cards, bank transfers (ACH), digital wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay).
  • Multi-currency support: Accept payments in 135+ currencies, settle in merchant's preferred currency.
  • Refunds & chargebacks: Full and partial refunds, chargeback dispute management.
  • Merchant payouts: Aggregate settled funds and disburse to merchant bank accounts on a configurable schedule.
  • Payment status tracking: Real-time status updates via webhooks and polling APIs.
  • Ledger & reporting: Complete financial ledger with audit trail for every money movement.
Non-Functional Requirements ▼
  • Exactly-once processing: A payment must never be charged twice, even under retries or network failures.
  • Strong consistency: Financial data requires linearizable reads/writes -- eventual consistency is unacceptable for balances.
  • PCI DSS compliance: Cardholder data must be tokenized and stored in an isolated, audited environment.
  • Auditability: Every state transition must be logged immutably for SOX / regulatory audits.
  • High availability: 99.99% uptime -- payment downtime directly costs merchants revenue.
  • Low latency: Payment authorization in < 2 seconds end-to-end.
  • Fault tolerance: Graceful degradation when downstream payment processors are unavailable.

2. Capacity Estimation

Scale Numbers ▼
Assumptions:
- $10B annual payment volume
- Average transaction: $50
- 200M transactions / year
- Peak: 3x average (Black Friday, holiday sales)

Transactions Per Second (TPS):
200M / (365 * 86,400) ≈ 6.3 TPS average
Peak: ~20 TPS (manageable, but consistency requirements make it hard)

Ledger Storage (5 years):
Each payment creates ~4 ledger entries (double-entry + fees)
200M * 4 * 5 = 4B ledger rows
~500 bytes/row → 2 TB raw ledger data

Payment Records:
200M * 5 years * 2 KB/record ≈ 2 TB

Key Insight: The challenge is NOT raw throughput (20 TPS is low). The challenge is correctness under failure, exactly-once semantics, and regulatory compliance. This is what makes it a staff-level problem.

5. Deep Dives

5a. Double-Entry Bookkeeping ▼

Why it matters: Every real payment platform (Stripe, Square, PayPal) uses double-entry bookkeeping internally. The principle is simple: every money movement creates exactly two entries -- a debit in one account and a credit in another. The sum of all debits must always equal the sum of all credits. This invariant is what makes financial systems auditable and self-verifying.

Why not just a single "balance" column? A single balance field is fragile: you lose the audit trail, you cannot reconstruct history, and you cannot detect corruption. With double-entry, you can independently verify every balance by summing the ledger entries.

5b. Idempotency -- The Most Critical Property ▼

Why it is critical: Network failures, client retries, and load balancer timeouts mean the same payment request can arrive multiple times. Without idempotency, a customer gets charged twice. This is the single most important correctness property in a payment system.

Idempotency Key Flow

sequenceDiagram participant C as Client participant GW as Payment Gateway participant Cache as Idempotency Store
(Redis + DB) participant PS as Payment Service participant PSP as Payment Processor C->>GW: POST /payments
Idempotency-Key: abc-123 GW->>Cache: Lookup key "abc-123" alt Key not found (first request) Cache-->>GW: NOT FOUND GW->>Cache: Store key "abc-123" = PROCESSING GW->>PS: Process payment PS->>PSP: Authorize + Capture PSP-->>PS: Success (ref: psp-456) PS-->>GW: Payment completed GW->>Cache: Update key "abc-123" = COMPLETED + response GW-->>C: 200 OK (payment result) else Key found with COMPLETED status (retry) Cache-->>GW: COMPLETED + cached response GW-->>C: 200 OK (cached result, no reprocessing) else Key found with PROCESSING status (concurrent) Cache-->>GW: PROCESSING GW-->>C: 409 Conflict (payment in progress) end

Implementation Details

-- Idempotency key table
CREATE TABLE idempotency_keys (
    key             VARCHAR(255) PRIMARY KEY,
    merchant_id     UUID NOT NULL,
    status          VARCHAR(20) NOT NULL,    -- PROCESSING, COMPLETED, FAILED
    request_hash    CHAR(64) NOT NULL,       -- SHA-256 of request body
    response_code   INT,
    response_body   JSONB,
    created_at      TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
    expires_at      TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL     -- TTL: 24-72 hours
);

-- Lookup is: WHERE key = ? AND merchant_id = ?
-- This prevents cross-merchant key collisions

Key design decisions:

  • Request hash: If the same idempotency key is sent with a different request body, reject it (400 Bad Request). This prevents misuse.
  • TTL: Keys expire after 24-72 hours. This bounds storage growth while covering reasonable retry windows.
  • Atomic status transitions: Use UPDATE ... WHERE status = 'PROCESSING' with row-level locking to prevent races.
  • PSP-level idempotency: Forward the idempotency key (or a derived key) to the PSP so that retries at the processor level are also safe.
Staff-Level Signal: Discuss the difference between at-most-once (drop duplicates) and exactly-once (idempotent processing). Explain that true exactly-once requires idempotency at every layer: client, gateway, service, AND external PSP.
5c. Payment State Machine ▼

Payments are modeled as a finite state machine. Every transition is explicit and audited. Invalid transitions are rejected, preventing data corruption.

stateDiagram-v2 [*] --> CREATED : Client submits payment CREATED --> PROCESSING : Risk check passed CREATED --> FAILED : Risk check rejected PROCESSING --> AUTHORIZED : PSP authorizes hold PROCESSING --> FAILED : PSP declines AUTHORIZED --> CAPTURED : Merchant captures funds AUTHORIZED --> CANCELLED : Auth voided (no capture) AUTHORIZED --> FAILED : Auth expired (typically 7 days) CAPTURED --> SETTLED : Settlement batch completes CAPTURED --> REFUND_PENDING : Refund initiated REFUND_PENDING --> REFUNDED : Refund confirmed by PSP REFUND_PENDING --> CAPTURED : Refund failed SETTLED --> REFUND_PENDING : Post-settlement refund FAILED --> [*] CANCELLED --> [*] REFUNDED --> [*] SETTLED --> [*]

State Transition Enforcement

# Python: State machine with explicit valid transitions
VALID_TRANSITIONS = {
    'CREATED':        ['PROCESSING', 'FAILED'],
    'PROCESSING':     ['AUTHORIZED', 'FAILED'],
    'AUTHORIZED':     ['CAPTURED', 'CANCELLED', 'FAILED'],
    'CAPTURED':       ['SETTLED', 'REFUND_PENDING'],
    'SETTLED':        ['REFUND_PENDING'],
    'REFUND_PENDING': ['REFUNDED', 'CAPTURED'],
}

def transition_payment(payment, new_status, reason=None):
    allowed = VALID_TRANSITIONS.get(payment.status, [])
    if new_status not in allowed:
        raise InvalidTransitionError(
            f"Cannot transition from {payment.status} to {new_status}"
        )

    old_status = payment.status
    payment.status = new_status
    payment.updated_at = datetime.utcnow()

    # Immutable audit log -- NEVER delete these
    AuditLog.create(
        payment_id=payment.id,
        from_status=old_status,
        to_status=new_status,
        reason=reason,
        timestamp=payment.updated_at,
        actor='system'
    )

Why separate Authorize and Capture? Authorization places a hold on the buyer's funds. Capture actually moves the money. This two-phase approach lets merchants verify inventory, check for fraud, or handle pre-orders before committing the charge. Hotels and car rentals rely heavily on auth-only flows.

Staff-Level Signal: Drawing the state machine proactively shows you understand that payments are not a simple request-response -- they are a long-lived process with multiple possible outcomes. Discuss edge cases: what happens if an authorization expires? What if a capture partially fails?
5d. Saga Pattern for Distributed Transactions ▼

The problem: A typical e-commerce checkout involves multiple services: Order Service (create order), Payment Service (charge money), and Inventory Service (reserve stock). You cannot use a traditional distributed transaction (2PC) because these are separate services, possibly with separate databases.

The solution: Use the Saga pattern -- a sequence of local transactions, each with a compensating action that undoes its effect if a later step fails.

Create Order (PENDING) O->>P: 2. Authorize Payment P->>P: Hold funds on buyer's card P-->>O: Authorization successful O->>I: 3. Reserve Inventory I->>I: Decrement stock count I-->>O: Reservation confirmed O->>P: 4. Capture Payment P->>L: Record ledger entries P-->>O: Capture confirmed O->>O: 5. Confirm Order (COMPLETED) Note over O,L: Failure + Compensation rect rgb(60, 20, 20) O->>I: 3. Reserve Inventory I-->>O: OUT OF STOCK O->>P: Compensate: Void Authorization P->>P: Release hold on buyer's card O->>O: Compensate: Cancel Order (FAILED) end

Choreography vs. Orchestration

ApproachHow It WorksProsCons
Choreography Each service emits events; other services react Loosely coupled, no single point of failure Hard to track overall saga state, debugging is painful
Orchestration Central coordinator drives the saga step by step Clear flow, easy to monitor and debug Coordinator is a single point of failure (mitigated with HA)

Recommendation for payments: Use orchestration. Payment flows must be deterministic and auditable. A central Payment Saga Coordinator makes it trivial to answer "what happened to payment X?" -- critical during incident response.

Staff-Level Signal: Explain why 2PC is impractical across microservices (latency, availability, heterogeneous data stores) and why sagas with compensating transactions are the industry-standard approach. Discuss the trade-off: sagas provide eventual consistency with explicit compensation, not atomicity.
5e. Reconciliation ▼

What is reconciliation? It is the process of verifying that your internal ledger matches external sources of truth: the PSP's transaction records and the bank's settlement statements. Discrepancies indicate bugs, fraud, or operational failures.

Three-Way Reconciliation

SourceContainsFrequency
Internal LedgerEvery debit/credit entry for every paymentReal-time
PSP Settlement ReportTransactions processed, fees deducted, net amountsDaily (T+1)
Bank StatementActual money movements in/out of bank accountsDaily (T+1 or T+2)

Reconciliation Process

# Simplified reconciliation logic
def reconcile_daily(date):
    internal = ledger_service.get_entries(date)
    psp_report = psp_client.download_settlement(date)
    bank_stmt = bank_client.get_statement(date)

    # Step 1: Match internal ledger to PSP report
    for psp_txn in psp_report.transactions:
        internal_txn = internal.find_by_psp_ref(psp_txn.reference)
        if not internal_txn:
            alert("MISSING_INTERNAL", psp_txn)  # PSP has it, we don't
        elif internal_txn.amount != psp_txn.amount:
            alert("AMOUNT_MISMATCH", internal_txn, psp_txn)

    # Step 2: Find orphaned internal records
    for txn in internal:
        if not psp_report.has_reference(txn.psp_reference):
            alert("MISSING_PSP", txn)  # We have it, PSP doesn't

    # Step 3: Verify bank settlement totals
    expected_net = psp_report.total_net
    actual_deposit = bank_stmt.find_deposit(psp_report.batch_id)
    if abs(expected_net - actual_deposit) > TOLERANCE:
        alert("BANK_MISMATCH", expected_net, actual_deposit)

Common discrepancies:

  • Timing differences: A payment captured at 11:59 PM may appear in tomorrow's PSP report.
  • Currency conversion: FX rates applied by the PSP may differ from your recorded rate.
  • Fee discrepancies: Tiered pricing means fees vary by volume, card type, and region.
  • Chargebacks: Disputes appear in bank statements before your system processes them.
Staff-Level Signal: Mentioning reconciliation unprompted is a strong differentiator. Most candidates focus only on the "happy path." Staff engineers know that the real complexity in payment systems lives in the reconciliation, dispute, and settlement layers.
5f. Fraud Detection & Risk Scoring ▼

The Risk Engine evaluates every payment in real-time before authorization. It combines rule-based checks with ML-based scoring.

Signals Used for Risk Scoring

  • Velocity checks: Number of transactions from the same card/IP/device in the last hour.
  • Geolocation: Does the IP location match the billing address? Is the buyer in a high-risk country?
  • Device fingerprinting: Browser, OS, screen resolution, timezone -- is this a known good device?
  • Transaction amount: Is this unusually large for this buyer or merchant?
  • Card BIN analysis: Prepaid cards and certain BIN ranges carry higher risk.
  • Behavioral signals: Time spent on checkout, copy-paste of card number (vs. typing).

Risk Decision Flow

def evaluate_risk(payment, context):
    score = 0.0

    # Rule-based checks (fast, deterministic)
    if context.velocity_1h > 10:
        score += 0.3
    if context.ip_country != payment.billing_country:
        score += 0.2
    if payment.amount > context.merchant_avg_amount * 5:
        score += 0.15

    # ML model (trained on historical fraud data)
    ml_score = fraud_model.predict(payment.features())
    score = 0.4 * score + 0.6 * ml_score  # weighted blend

    # Decision
    if score > 0.85:
        return RiskDecision.BLOCK
    elif score > 0.6:
        return RiskDecision.CHALLENGE  # 3D Secure / OTP
    else:
        return RiskDecision.ALLOW

3D Secure (3DS): When risk is medium, trigger a 3DS challenge (e.g., SMS OTP or biometric verification). This shifts liability for fraud from the merchant to the card issuer -- a critical commercial consideration.

5g. PCI DSS Compliance & Tokenization ▼

PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) governs how cardholder data is stored, processed, and transmitted. Violating PCI DSS can result in fines of $5K-$100K/month and loss of card processing privileges.

Tokenization Architecture

The key strategy is minimizing PCI scope. Raw card numbers (PANs) never touch your main application. Instead:

  1. The client sends card data directly to the Token Vault (via a Stripe.js-style SDK or iframe).
  2. The Token Vault encrypts the PAN using HSM-backed keys and returns a token (e.g., tok_abc123).
  3. Your Payment Service only ever handles tokens -- never raw card data.
  4. When charging, the Token Vault decrypts the PAN and sends it to the PSP over a secured channel.

Network Isolation

|  Main VPC (non-PCI scope)                        |

|  [Payment Gateway] [Payment Service] [Ledger]    |

+---------|----------------------------------------+

Key PCI requirements for the interview:

  • Cardholder data encrypted at rest (AES-256) and in transit (TLS 1.2+).
  • Access logging for every PCI-scope system -- who accessed what, when.
  • Network segmentation: PCI systems on separate VLANs/VPCs with firewall rules.
  • Regular penetration testing and vulnerability scanning.
  • No storage of CVV/CVC after authorization (even encrypted).
5h. Multi-Currency Support ▼

Multi-currency adds significant complexity. The core question is: when do you convert currencies?

Conversion Strategies

StrategyDescriptionProsCons
Charge-time conversion Convert to merchant's currency at the moment of charge Merchant sees a predictable amount Buyer may see unexpected amounts due to FX markup
Settlement-time conversion Store in original currency, convert during settlement More accurate FX rates, simpler charge flow Merchant faces FX risk until settlement
Multi-currency ledger Maintain separate ledger accounts per currency No conversion needed until payout; accurate bookkeeping More complex ledger, need FX entries for cross-currency payouts

Best practice: Use a multi-currency ledger. Record every transaction in its original currency. Create explicit FX ledger entries when converting for payouts. This keeps the ledger accurate and auditable.

5i. Retry Handling: When to Retry vs. When to Fail ▼

Retry logic in payment systems is nuanced. Blindly retrying can cause double charges. Never retrying can cause lost revenue. The key is classifying failures correctly.

Failure Classification

Failure TypeExampleRetryable?Action
Network timeoutPSP did not respond in 10sYes (with idempotency key)Retry up to 3x with exponential backoff
PSP 5xx errorPSP internal server errorYesRetry with backoff; fall back to secondary PSP if persistent
Soft decline"Insufficient funds", "Try again"MaybeRetry after delay (card may have funds later), or prompt buyer

Critical Rule: Unknown State Is the Hardest

When a network timeout occurs, you do NOT know if the PSP processed the payment. You must:

  1. Query the PSP using your idempotency key to check the status.
  2. If the PSP confirms it was processed, record it as successful.
  3. If the PSP has no record, retry with the same idempotency key.
  4. If the PSP is unreachable, mark the payment as UNKNOWN and reconcile later.
async def charge_with_retry(payment, max_retries=3):
    for attempt in range(max_retries):
        try:
            result = await psp.charge(
                amount=payment.amount,
                currency=payment.currency,
                token=payment.token_id,
                idempotency_key=payment.idempotency_key
            )
            return result
        except NetworkTimeout:
            # Unknown state -- check before retrying
            status = await psp.query_payment(payment.idempotency_key)
            if status == 'COMPLETED':
                return status
            elif status == 'NOT_FOUND':
                await asyncio.sleep(2 ** attempt)  # exponential backoff
                continue
            else:
                raise UnknownPaymentStateError(payment.id)
        except HardDecline as e:
            # Never retry hard declines
            raise PaymentFailedError(e.reason)

    # All retries exhausted
    payment.status = 'UNKNOWN'
    reconciliation_queue.enqueue(payment.id)
    raise PaymentRetryExhaustedError(payment.id)

6. Failure Scenarios

What Happens When Things Go Wrong ▼

Scenario 1: Payment Provider Down

Situation: Your primary PSP (e.g., Visa network) is experiencing an outage.

Mitigation:

  • Multi-PSP routing: Maintain connections to 2-3 PSPs. If one is down, route to another. This requires abstracting PSP communication behind an adapter interface.
  • Circuit breaker: After N consecutive failures, open the circuit. Stop sending traffic to the failing PSP. Periodically probe to detect recovery.
  • Graceful degradation: If all card processors are down, still accept wallet payments or bank transfers. Show the buyer available payment methods dynamically.

Scenario 2: Double Charge

Situation: A network glitch causes the client to retry, and idempotency protection fails (e.g., different idempotency keys used).

Prevention:

  • Server-generated idempotency keys tied to the order ID (not client-generated).
  • Unique constraint on (merchant_id, order_reference) in the payments table.
  • Reconciliation job detects duplicate charges within minutes.

Recovery: Automatic refund of the duplicate charge, alert to on-call engineer, incident review.

Scenario 3: Partial Failure in Saga

Situation: Payment is captured but inventory reservation fails (item sold out after payment).

Recovery:

  • Saga coordinator triggers compensating transaction: automatic refund.
  • Order status updated to FAILED with reason "out of stock."
  • Notification sent to buyer: "Payment refunded -- item unavailable."

Key principle: The system must never be in a state where money is taken but no product is delivered (or refund issued). Compensation must be guaranteed.

Scenario 4: Ledger Database Failure

Situation: The ledger DB is temporarily unavailable.

Impact: This is catastrophic -- you CANNOT process payments without recording them in the ledger. Unlike most services, you do NOT "proceed without logging."

Mitigation:

  • Synchronous replication with automatic failover (PostgreSQL streaming replication or Aurora Multi-AZ).
  • If the ledger is truly down, stop accepting payments. Return 503 to clients. This is the correct behavior -- processing payments you cannot record is worse than downtime.
  • Write-ahead log to durable queue (Kafka) as a last resort, with strict reconciliation on recovery.
Staff-Level Signal: The decision to reject payments when the ledger is down (rather than "queue and process later") demonstrates understanding that financial correctness trumps availability. This is the opposite of most distributed systems advice and shows mature judgment.

8. Scaling & Performance Considerations

How to Scale a Payment System ▼

Payment systems scale differently from typical web applications. The bottleneck is rarely raw throughput -- it is consistency and correctness under concurrent access.

Database Scaling Strategy

  • Vertical scaling first: A well-tuned PostgreSQL instance can handle 10K+ TPS. Payment systems often stay on a single primary longer than you might expect, because horizontal scaling introduces consistency challenges.
  • Read replicas: Reporting, analytics, and dashboard queries go to replicas. Payment processing always hits the primary.
  • Sharding (when necessary): Shard by merchant_id. All payments for a merchant live on the same shard, enabling efficient queries and maintaining per-merchant consistency.
  • Ledger partitioning: Partition ledger_entries by created_at (monthly). Old partitions become read-only and can be moved to cheaper storage.

Caching

  • Idempotency keys: Redis with TTL (24-72 hours). Hot path for deduplication.
  • Merchant config: Cache merchant settings (fee rates, payout schedules) in local memory. Refresh every few minutes.
  • Do NOT cache: Payment status, ledger balances, or anything where stale reads cause financial errors.

Async Processing

  • Settlement: Batch job, runs daily. No need for real-time processing.
  • Webhooks: Async via Kafka. Retry with exponential backoff. Separate webhook delivery from the payment hot path.
  • Reconciliation: Offline batch job, runs after PSP/bank reports are available (T+1).

9. Common Follow-Up Questions

Questions Interviewers Ask ▼

Q: How do you ensure exactly-once payment processing?

A: Idempotency keys at every layer. The client sends a unique key, the gateway deduplicates against a persistent store, and the same key is forwarded to the PSP. Combined with a state machine that rejects invalid transitions, this ensures each payment is processed exactly once.

Q: Why not use a distributed database like Cassandra for the ledger?

A: Financial ledgers require strong consistency (linearizable reads). Cassandra offers eventual consistency by default, which means you could read stale balances. PostgreSQL (or CockroachDB/Spanner for distributed strong consistency) is the right choice because they guarantee that a read after a write sees the write.

Q: How do you handle chargebacks?

A: Chargebacks are initiated by the buyer's bank. The PSP notifies us via webhook. We create a chargeback record linked to the original payment, debit the merchant's account in the ledger, and provide a dispute management interface for merchants to submit evidence. The typical window is 120 days.

Q: How do you handle a payment that is stuck in PROCESSING?

A: A background reaper job scans for payments in PROCESSING state older than a threshold (e.g., 15 minutes). It queries the PSP for the payment status. If the PSP confirms success or failure, the state is updated. If the PSP has no record, the payment is marked FAILED and the merchant is notified.

Q: What happens during a database failover?

A: With synchronous replication, the replica is guaranteed to have all committed transactions. During failover (typically 15-30 seconds for managed databases like Aurora), in-flight payments will time out. Clients retry with idempotency keys. The new primary picks up from the last committed state. No data is lost, no payments are duplicated.

Q: How would you add support for subscriptions / recurring payments?

A: Add a Subscription Service that stores billing schedules. A cron-based scheduler creates payment intents at the appropriate intervals. Each recurring charge uses a deterministic idempotency key (e.g., sub_{id}_period_{date}) to prevent double-billing. Handle card expiration by supporting card-on-file updates and retry logic for soft declines.

Q: How do you test a payment system?

A: PSPs provide sandbox environments with test card numbers that trigger specific responses (success, decline, timeout). Internally, use property-based testing to verify ledger invariants (sum of debits = sum of credits), chaos engineering to inject failures (kill PSP connections mid-transaction), and shadow traffic testing against production data.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Treating payments as a simple CRUD service -- "just store it in a database."
  • Not mentioning idempotency or exactly-once semantics.
  • Using eventual consistency for financial data without acknowledging the risks.
  • Ignoring PCI compliance entirely.
  • No mention of reconciliation or what happens when things go wrong.

Differentiators for Staff-Level

  • Drawing the payment state machine without being asked.
  • Explaining double-entry bookkeeping and why a single balance field is insufficient.
  • Discussing the tension between availability and consistency in financial systems.
  • Mentioning three-way reconciliation (ledger vs. PSP vs. bank).
  • Understanding when to STOP accepting payments (ledger down) rather than degrading gracefully.
