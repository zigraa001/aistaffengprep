1. Why Reliability Matters

The Business Case for Reliability

Reliability is not a nice-to-have feature -- it is the foundation on which every other feature depends. A system that is unavailable cannot serve users, process revenue, or fulfill its purpose. At staff level, you are expected to reason about reliability quantitatively, not just say "we need high availability."

The Nines of Availability

Availability is commonly expressed as "nines." Each additional nine represents a 10x reduction in allowed downtime. The table below shows what each level means in practice:

Availability Downtime / Year Downtime / Month Downtime / Week Typical Use Case
99% (two nines) 3.65 days 7.31 hours 1.68 hours Batch / internal tools
99.9% (three nines) 8.77 hours 43.8 minutes 10.1 minutes SaaS applications
99.95% 4.38 hours 21.9 minutes 5.04 minutes Cloud provider APIs
99.99% (four nines) 52.6 minutes 4.38 minutes 1.01 minutes Payment / auth systems
99.999% (five nines) 5.26 minutes 26.3 seconds 6.05 seconds Telecom, 911 systems

Cost of Downtime at Scale

Downtime is not abstract -- it has real financial and reputational consequences:

  • Amazon: Estimated to lose ~$220,000 per minute of downtime (based on 2023 revenue figures)
  • Google: A 5-minute outage in 2013 reduced global internet traffic by 40%
  • Facebook: The October 2021 outage lasted ~6 hours and cost an estimated $100M+ in revenue
  • Delta Airlines: A 2016 systems outage cost $150M in cancelled flights and rebooking

Beyond direct revenue loss, downtime erodes user trust. Studies show that 88% of users are less likely to return to a site after a bad experience, and reputational damage can persist long after systems are restored.

Staff-Level Signal

In interviews, don't just say "we need 99.99% availability." Explain why your chosen target is right for the problem. A logging analytics dashboard does not need the same availability as a payment gateway. Staff engineers reason about the cost-benefit tradeoff: each additional nine of availability is roughly 10x more expensive to achieve, so you must justify the investment.

Compound Availability

When components are chained in series, overall availability is the product of individual availabilities. When components are in parallel (redundant), availability increases.

Series:   A_total = A1 x A2 x A3
  Example: 99.9% x 99.9% x 99.9% = 99.7% (three services in series)

Parallel: A_total = 1 - (1 - A1) x (1 - A2)
  Example: 1 - (1 - 0.999) x (1 - 0.999) = 99.9999% (two redundant instances)

This is why redundancy is critical for high-availability systems, and why you should identify the weakest link in any dependency chain. A system is only as reliable as its least reliable serial dependency.

2. SLIs, SLOs, and SLAs

The Google SRE Approach to Reliability

Google's Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) framework popularized a structured approach to reliability that has become the industry standard. The three pillars are SLIs, SLOs, and SLAs -- each building on the previous.

Definitions

SLI (Service Level Indicator)

A quantitative measure of some aspect of the level of service provided. SLIs are the raw metrics you measure.

Examples: Request latency (p99), error rate (% of 5xx), throughput (requests/sec), availability (% of successful probes)

SLO (Service Level Objective)

A target value or range for an SLI. SLOs are internal goals your team commits to.

Examples: "99.9% of requests complete in <200ms", "Error rate <0.1% over a rolling 30-day window"

SLA (Service Level Agreement)

A contractual obligation with consequences (credits, penalties) if the SLO is not met. SLAs are always looser than SLOs.

Examples: "99.9% monthly uptime or customer receives 10% credit" (AWS, Azure, GCP all publish SLAs)

How to Choose Good SLIs

Good SLIs share several characteristics. They should be:

  • User-facing: Measure what the user experiences, not internal system health. A user cares about "did the page load in under 2 seconds?" not "is CPU below 70%."
  • Proportional: Expressed as a ratio (good events / total events) so they naturally produce a percentage.
  • Actionable: When an SLI degrades, the team can identify and fix the underlying issue.
Service Type Key SLIs Typical SLO
User-facing HTTP API Availability, latency (p50, p99), error rate 99.9% success, p99 < 300ms
Data pipeline Freshness, throughput, completeness Data < 10 min stale, 99.9% records processed
Storage system Durability, availability, latency 99.999999999% durability (11 nines), 99.99% availability
Streaming / real-time End-to-end latency, message loss rate p99 < 500ms, < 0.01% message loss

Error Budgets

An error budget is the complement of your SLO. If your SLO is 99.9% availability over a 30-day window, your error budget is 0.1% -- approximately 43 minutes of downtime per month. The error budget is the most powerful concept in SRE because it turns reliability into a negotiation:

  • Budget remaining: The team can deploy risky changes, run experiments, and ship faster.
  • Budget exhausted: The team freezes feature work and focuses on reliability improvements (postmortems, automation, hardening).

This creates an elegant feedback loop: product teams want to ship fast but also want to keep the error budget healthy, so they naturally invest in testing, rollback mechanisms, and canary deployments.

Staff-Level Signal

When you propose an SLO in an interview, also mention how you would enforce it. Error budgets are meaningless if nobody acts on them. Describe the error budget policy: who gets paged, when feature freezes kick in, and how the budget is reviewed weekly. This shows organizational maturity beyond just technical design.

3. The Three Pillars of Observability

Observability is the ability to understand a system's internal state by examining its external outputs. The three pillars -- metrics, logs, and traces -- provide complementary views of system behavior.

3a. Metrics

Metrics are numeric time-series data that describe the state of a system over time. They are highly compressible, cheap to store at scale, and excellent for dashboards and alerting. Tools include Prometheus, Datadog, CloudWatch, and Grafana for visualization.

The RED Method (Request-Scoped)

Designed by Tom Wilkie (Grafana Labs), the RED method focuses on the experience of each request:

  • Rate: Number of requests per second your service is handling
  • Errors: Number of those requests that fail (5xx, timeouts, etc.)
  • Duration: Distribution of how long those requests take (p50, p95, p99)

RED is ideal for request-driven services (APIs, web servers, microservices). It answers: "Are users having a good experience?"

The USE Method (Resource-Scoped)

Created by Brendan Gregg (Netflix), the USE method focuses on infrastructure health:

  • Utilization: Percentage of time the resource is busy (CPU at 80%, disk at 60%)
  • Saturation: Amount of work queued that the resource cannot serve (queue depth, memory swap)
  • Errors: Count of error events on the resource (disk errors, NIC packet drops)

USE is ideal for infrastructure components (CPU, memory, disk, network). It answers: "Which resource is the bottleneck?"

The Four Golden Signals (Google SRE)

Google SRE combines both approaches into four golden signals that every service should monitor:

Staff-Level Signal

Know when to use histograms vs. summaries: histograms can be aggregated across instances (use histogram_quantile() in PromQL), while summaries compute quantiles per instance and cannot be meaningfully aggregated. For most use cases, prefer histograms. Also, discuss metric cardinality: adding high-cardinality labels (like user_id) to Prometheus metrics is a common anti-pattern that causes storage and memory explosions.

3b. Logging

Logs provide detailed, event-level context about what happened and why. While metrics tell you something is wrong, logs help you understand why it is wrong. The challenge is managing logs at scale -- a service handling 100k RPS can produce terabytes of logs per day.

Structured Logging

Always use structured logging (JSON or key-value pairs) instead of free-form text. Structured logs are parseable, searchable, and can be indexed efficiently.

// BAD: Unstructured log
logger.info("User 12345 placed order 67890 for $99.99")

// GOOD: Structured log (JSON)
logger.info({
  "event": "order_placed",
  "user_id": "12345",
  "order_id": "67890",
  "amount_usd": 99.99,
  "payment_method": "credit_card",
  "trace_id": "abc-def-123",
  "timestamp": "2025-03-15T14:30:00Z",
  "service": "order-service",
  "environment": "production"
})

Centralized Log Aggregation Architecture

At scale, logs must be shipped from many services to a centralized system for search and analysis. The ELK stack (Elasticsearch, Logstash, Kibana) and its alternatives (Loki, Splunk, Datadog Logs) are industry standards.

flowchart LR subgraph Services S1[Service A] --> F1[Filebeat / Fluentd] S2[Service B] --> F2[Filebeat / Fluentd] S3[Service C] --> F3[Filebeat / Fluentd] end subgraph Ingestion F1 --> K[Kafka / Buffer] F2 --> K F3 --> K K --> L[Logstash / Vector] end subgraph Storage & Query L --> ES[Elasticsearch / Loki] ES --> KB[Kibana / Grafana] end subgraph Alerting ES --> AL[Alert Rules] AL --> PG[PagerDuty / Slack] end

Key architectural decisions:

  • Buffer layer (Kafka): Decouples log producers from consumers, preventing log storms from overwhelming the indexing layer. This is critical at scale.
  • Agents (Filebeat/Fluentd): Lightweight processes on each host that tail log files and forward them. They handle backpressure and retries.
  • Retention policy: Hot storage (fast SSD, 7-14 days), warm storage (slower disk, 30-90 days), cold/archive (S3/GCS, 1+ year). Balance cost against investigation needs.
  • Sampling: For ultra-high-volume services, sample DEBUG-level logs (e.g., 1%) while keeping all ERROR logs.

Staff-Level Signal

Discuss log cost management in interviews. At companies like Uber and Netflix, logging costs can exceed $1M/month. Strategies include: log-level sampling, dropping low-value fields before indexing, tiered retention, and log-based metrics (extract counters from logs instead of storing every event). Also mention correlation: every log line should carry a trace_id so you can correlate logs with traces and metrics.

3c. Distributed Tracing

Distributed tracing tracks a single request as it traverses multiple services. While metrics show aggregate behavior and logs show individual events, traces show the causal chain of a request across the entire system. This is essential for debugging latency issues in microservice architectures.

Core Concepts

  • Trace: The end-to-end journey of a single request, identified by a unique trace ID.
  • Span: A named, timed operation within a trace. Each service creates one or more spans. Spans form a tree/DAG.
  • Context Propagation: Passing the trace ID and parent span ID between services via HTTP headers (e.g., traceparent, X-B3-TraceId) or gRPC metadata.
  • Baggage: Key-value pairs propagated with the trace for cross-cutting concerns (e.g., user tier, experiment ID).

OpenTelemetry (OTel)

OpenTelemetry is the CNCF standard that unifies tracing, metrics, and logging instrumentation. It provides:

  • Language-specific SDKs (Python, Java, Go, Node.js, .NET)
  • Auto-instrumentation for common frameworks (Spring, Express, Django)
  • The OTLP protocol for exporting telemetry data
  • The OpenTelemetry Collector for receiving, processing, and exporting data to backends (Jaeger, Zipkin, Datadog, Honeycomb)

Sampling Strategies

At high traffic volumes, tracing every request is prohibitively expensive. Sampling strategies include:

  • Head-based sampling: Decide at the entry point whether to trace. Simple (e.g., sample 1% of requests), but you miss interesting traces.
  • Tail-based sampling: Collect all spans, then decide after the trace completes whether to keep it. Keeps error traces and slow traces but requires buffering all spans temporarily. More expensive but far more useful.
  • Priority sampling: Always trace error requests, requests above a latency threshold, or requests matching certain attributes (e.g., specific user IDs for debugging).
# OpenTelemetry Python - basic instrumentation
from opentelemetry import trace
from opentelemetry.sdk.trace import TracerProvider
from opentelemetry.sdk.trace.export import BatchSpanProcessor
from opentelemetry.exporter.otlp.proto.grpc.trace_exporter import OTLPSpanExporter

# Setup
provider = TracerProvider()
processor = BatchSpanProcessor(OTLPSpanExporter(endpoint="otel-collector:4317"))
provider.add_span_processor(processor)
trace.set_tracer_provider(provider)

tracer = trace.get_tracer("order-service")

# Create a span
with tracer.start_as_current_span("process_order") as span:
    span.set_attribute("order.id", order_id)
    span.set_attribute("order.amount", 99.99)

    # Child span for database call
    with tracer.start_as_current_span("db_insert_order") as db_span:
        db_span.set_attribute("db.system", "postgresql")
        db_span.set_attribute("db.statement", "INSERT INTO orders ...")
        result = db.insert(order)

    # Child span for downstream service call
    with tracer.start_as_current_span("call_payment_service") as pay_span:
        pay_span.set_attribute("rpc.service", "PaymentService")
        response = payment_client.charge(order)

Staff-Level Signal

In interviews, mention tail-based sampling as the preferred approach for production systems. Explain why: head-based sampling at 1% means you only have a 1% chance of capturing any given error trace. With tail-based sampling via the OTel Collector, you keep 100% of error traces and interesting outliers while discarding the majority of healthy traces. Also discuss the cost of context propagation: it adds a few hundred bytes of headers to every inter-service call, which is negligible for HTTP but matters for high-frequency gRPC streams.

4. Alerting

Effective Alerting Strategy

Alerting is the bridge between observability data and human action. Done well, alerts catch real problems before users notice. Done poorly, alert fatigue desensitizes on-call engineers and real incidents get missed.

Alert Fatigue: The Silent Killer

Alert fatigue occurs when engineers receive so many alerts that they begin to ignore them. This is extremely dangerous -- it directly contributed to major incidents at companies like Knight Capital ($440M loss in 45 minutes, 2012).

Signs of alert fatigue:

  • On-call engineer receives >5 pages per shift that require no action
  • Alerts are routinely acknowledged and immediately closed without investigation
  • >30% of alerts are false positives
  • Team maintains a "known issues" doc of alerts to ignore

Symptom-Based vs. Cause-Based Alerts

ApproachAlert OnExamplePros / Cons
Symptom-based (preferred) User-visible impact "Error rate > 1% for 5 min" Low noise, catches unknown causes. Slower to detect.
Cause-based Specific known failure "Database CPU > 90%" Fast detection for known issues. Miss novel failures, high noise.

Best practice: Page on symptoms (user impact), use cause-based alerts as non-paging informational signals. Google SRE recommends: "If a human must take action, it should be because users are affected, not because a metric crossed a threshold."

Severity Levels and Escalation

SeverityCriteriaResponseNotification
P0 / SEV1 Service down, major revenue impact, data loss risk Immediate (wake people up) PagerDuty, phone call, incident channel
P1 / SEV2 Degraded performance, partial outage, SLO at risk Within 30 minutes PagerDuty, Slack alert channel
P2 / SEV3 Non-critical feature broken, workaround exists Next business day Slack, email, ticket created
P3 / SEV4 Minor issue, cosmetic, low user impact Backlog / sprint planning Ticket only

Runbooks

Every paging alert should link to a runbook -- a document that tells the on-call engineer exactly what to do. A good runbook includes:

  • What this alert means: Brief explanation of the symptom and potential causes
  • Impact: What users experience when this fires
  • Diagnostic steps: Dashboards to check, queries to run, commands to execute
  • Mitigation steps: How to restore service quickly (rollback, failover, restart, scale up)
  • Escalation path: Who to contact if the runbook steps do not resolve the issue
  • Previous incidents: Links to relevant postmortems

Staff-Level Signal

When discussing alerting in an interview, mention multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting (from the Google SRE book). Instead of a simple threshold, you alert when the error rate would exhaust the error budget at the current burn rate. For example: alert on a 14.4x burn rate over 1 hour (consuming the 30-day budget in ~2 days) as critical, and a 3x burn rate over 6 hours as a warning. This dramatically reduces false positives while catching real problems quickly.

5. Chaos Engineering

Proactive Failure Testing

Chaos engineering is the discipline of experimenting on a system to build confidence in its ability to withstand turbulent conditions in production. Instead of waiting for failures to find you, you proactively inject them under controlled conditions.

Netflix: Chaos Monkey and the Simian Army

Netflix pioneered chaos engineering out of necessity. When they migrated to AWS in 2011, they needed to ensure their services could handle the inherent unreliability of cloud infrastructure.

  • Chaos Monkey: Randomly terminates VM instances in production during business hours. Forces engineers to design services that tolerate instance failure.
  • Latency Monkey: Injects artificial delays into RESTful client-server communications to simulate degradation.
  • Conformity Monkey: Finds instances that do not adhere to best practices and shuts them down.
  • Chaos Gorilla: Simulates an entire AWS Availability Zone failure.
  • Chaos Kong: Simulates an entire AWS Region failure (the ultimate test).

Principles of Chaos Engineering

  1. Build a hypothesis around steady-state behavior: Define what "normal" looks like using SLIs (e.g., "p99 latency stays under 200ms, error rate under 0.1%").
  2. Vary real-world events: Inject failures that actually happen: server crashes, network partitions, disk full, clock skew, DNS failures.
  3. Run experiments in production: Staging environments often do not match production fidelity. Start small (canary), but production is where you learn the most.
  4. Automate experiments to run continuously: One-off tests provide a snapshot; continuous experiments catch regressions.
  5. Minimize blast radius: Use canary groups, feature flags, and automatic rollback to limit the impact of experiments.

Game Days

A game day is a planned chaos engineering exercise where the team simulates a major incident scenario. It tests not just the technical systems but also the human response process -- communication, coordination, decision-making under pressure.

Google calls their version "DiRT" (Disaster Recovery Testing). They regularly simulate scenarios like:

  • Loss of an entire data center
  • Key person unavailable ("what if the DBA is on vacation?")
  • Simultaneous failure of multiple independent systems
  • Catastrophic data corruption requiring restore from backup

Real-World Examples

Amazon: Teams are required to have "correction of error" (COE) documents and regularly test their systems' resilience. AWS GameDay events bring teams together to compete on incident response.

Google DiRT: In one DiRT exercise, Google deliberately made the corporate headquarters "unavailable" to test whether critical services could be managed by distributed teams. They discovered that several manual processes depended on physical access to the building.

Slack: Runs "Disasterpiece Theater" exercises where they simulate production failures and evaluate both the technical response and the incident management process. They credit these exercises with reducing mean time to recovery (MTTR) by over 50%.

Staff-Level Signal

In system design interviews, proactively mention chaos engineering when discussing reliability. For example: "We would run chaos experiments injecting 500ms latency between the API and database to validate our circuit breaker configuration and ensure the timeout is set correctly." This demonstrates that you think about systems holistically -- not just the happy path, but how they fail. Staff engineers own the reliability story end-to-end.

6. Incident Management

The Incident Lifecycle

How an organization responds to incidents is as important as how it prevents them. Staff engineers are expected to lead incident response and drive improvements from postmortems.

Incident Commander Role

The Incident Commander (IC) is the single point of authority during an incident. The IC does not debug the issue -- their job is to coordinate the response:

  • Delegate tasks: Assign people to debugging, communication, and mitigation workstreams
  • Make decisions: When there are competing mitigation strategies, the IC decides
  • Communicate status: Regular updates to stakeholders (every 15-30 min for SEV1)
  • Manage scope: Prevent scope creep -- focus on mitigation first, root cause later
  • Call for help: Escalate to additional teams when needed

Blameless Postmortems

A postmortem is a structured review of an incident conducted after resolution. The goal is to learn and improve, not to assign blame. Blameless culture is essential: if people fear punishment, they will hide mistakes and the organization cannot learn.

Staff-Level Signal

Staff engineers do not just participate in postmortems -- they drive systemic improvements across teams. 7. Reliability Patterns

Circuit Breaker Pattern

The circuit breaker pattern prevents a service from repeatedly calling a failing downstream dependency. Like an electrical circuit breaker, it "trips" when failures exceed a threshold, stopping calls and returning a fallback response until the downstream recovers.

State Machine

(e.g., 5 failures in 10s) Open --> HalfOpen : Timeout expires
(e.g., after 30s) HalfOpen --> Closed : Probe request succeeds HalfOpen --> Open : Probe request fails Closed --> Closed : Success / failure below threshold note right of Closed : Normal operation
All requests pass through note right of Open : Fail fast
Return fallback immediately note right of HalfOpen : Testing recovery
Allow one probe request 
// Circuit Breaker implementation (Python)
import time
from enum import Enum

class State(Enum):
    CLOSED = "closed"
    OPEN = "open"
    HALF_OPEN = "half_open"

class CircuitBreaker:
    def __init__(self, failure_threshold=5, recovery_timeout=30, expected_exception=Exception):
        self.failure_threshold = failure_threshold
        self.recovery_timeout = recovery_timeout
        self.expected_exception = expected_exception
        self.state = State.CLOSED
        self.failure_count = 0
        self.last_failure_time = None
        self.success_count = 0

    def call(self, func, *args, **kwargs):
        if self.state == State.OPEN:
            if time.time() - self.last_failure_time > self.recovery_timeout:
                self.state = State.HALF_OPEN
                self.success_count = 0
            else:
                raise CircuitBreakerOpenError("Circuit is OPEN - failing fast")

        try:
            result = func(*args, **kwargs)
            self._on_success()
            return result
        except self.expected_exception as e:
            self._on_failure()
            raise

    def _on_success(self):
        if self.state == State.HALF_OPEN:
            self.success_count += 1
            if self.success_count >= 3:  # require 3 consecutive successes
                self.state = State.CLOSED
                self.failure_count = 0
        else:
            self.failure_count = 0

    def _on_failure(self):
        self.failure_count += 1
        self.last_failure_time = time.time()
        if self.failure_count >= self.failure_threshold:
            self.state = State.OPEN

Real-world usage: Netflix Hystrix (now in maintenance mode), Resilience4j (Java), Polly (.NET), gobreaker (Go). Service meshes like Istio and Linkerd can implement circuit breakers at the infrastructure layer without code changes.

Retry with Exponential Backoff + Jitter

Retries handle transient failures, but naive retries (immediate, fixed interval) can cause a "retry storm" that overwhelms recovering services. Exponential backoff spaces out retries, and jitter decorrelates them across clients.

import random
import time

def retry_with_backoff(func, max_retries=5, base_delay=1.0, max_delay=60.0):
    """Retry with exponential backoff and full jitter."""
    for attempt in range(max_retries):
        try:
            return func()
        except TransientError as e:
            if attempt == max_retries - 1:
                raise  # exhausted retries

            # Exponential backoff: 1s, 2s, 4s, 8s, 16s...
            exp_delay = min(base_delay * (2 ** attempt), max_delay)

            # Full jitter: random value between 0 and exp_delay
            # This prevents thundering herd when many clients retry
            jitter_delay = random.uniform(0, exp_delay)

            print(f"Attempt {attempt + 1} failed, retrying in {jitter_delay:.2f}s")
            time.sleep(jitter_delay)

Why jitter matters: Without jitter, if 1000 clients all fail at the same time, they all retry at the same time (1s, 2s, 4s...), creating synchronized spikes. With full jitter, retries are spread uniformly across the backoff window, reducing peak load on the recovering service by orders of magnitude.

Key design decisions:

  • Which errors to retry: Only retry transient errors (503, 429, network timeouts). Never retry 400 Bad Request or 401 Unauthorized -- those will never succeed.
  • Idempotency: Retries are only safe if the operation is idempotent. For non-idempotent operations (e.g., payment), use idempotency keys.
  • Retry budgets: Limit retries to a percentage of total requests (e.g., Google recommends 10%). If >10% of requests are retries, something is fundamentally wrong.
Bulkhead Isolation

Named after watertight compartments in ships, the bulkhead pattern isolates failures so that a problem in one component does not cascade to others. Without bulkheads, a slow downstream service can consume all your thread pool or connection pool resources, causing the entire application to become unresponsive.

Implementation Strategies

  • Thread pool isolation: Each downstream dependency gets its own thread pool. If the payment service is slow, only payment threads are blocked; the catalog service continues normally.
  • Semaphore isolation: Lightweight alternative using counting semaphores. Less overhead than thread pools but no timeout capability.
  • Process isolation: Run different workloads in separate containers or VMs. Heaviest isolation but simplest to reason about.
  • Connection pool partitioning: Divide database connection pools by workload type (e.g., 60% for reads, 30% for writes, 10% for admin queries).
// Java - Bulkhead with separate thread pools (Resilience4j)
BulkheadConfig paymentBulkhead = BulkheadConfig.custom()
    .maxConcurrentCalls(25)      // max 25 concurrent calls to payment
    .maxWaitDuration(Duration.ofMillis(500))  // wait 500ms for a slot
    .build();

BulkheadConfig inventoryBulkhead = BulkheadConfig.custom()
    .maxConcurrentCalls(50)      // inventory gets more capacity
    .maxWaitDuration(Duration.ofMillis(200))
    .build();

// If payment is slow, only 25 threads are blocked
// Inventory, catalog, and other services continue normally
Timeouts, Deadline Propagation & Graceful Degradation

Timeouts

Every network call must have a timeout. Without timeouts, a hung downstream service causes callers to wait indefinitely, consuming resources and cascading failures upstream. Set timeouts based on the dependency's expected latency distribution:

  • Connect timeout: How long to wait for TCP connection (typically 1-5s)
  • Read timeout: How long to wait for a response after connecting (varies by dependency)
  • Rule of thumb: Set timeout to p999 (99.9th percentile) of the dependency's response time, plus some buffer

Deadline Propagation

In a chain of microservices (A calls B calls C calls D), each service should propagate the remaining deadline rather than setting independent timeouts. Otherwise, service D might start processing a request that A has already timed out on.

# gRPC deadline propagation example
# Service A sets a 5-second deadline
# When B receives the request, 1 second has elapsed
# B propagates a 4-second deadline to C
# When C receives it, 0.5 seconds more have elapsed
# C propagates a 3.5-second deadline to D

# In gRPC (Python), deadlines propagate automatically:
stub.ProcessOrder(request, timeout=5.0)

# In HTTP, propagate via header:
# X-Request-Deadline: 2025-03-15T14:30:05.000Z
remaining = deadline - time.now()
if remaining <= 0:
    return 504  # already expired, don't call downstream

Graceful Degradation

When a dependency fails, serve a degraded but usable experience instead of a hard error:

  • Netflix: If the personalized recommendation service is down, show trending/popular content instead
  • Amazon: If the review service is slow, show the product page without reviews
  • Twitter/X: If the like-count service is unavailable, show the tweet without engagement metrics

Load Shedding

When a system is overloaded, it is better to reject some requests cleanly (returning 503) than to accept all of them and serve all of them slowly. Load shedding is the intentional rejection of excess load to protect the service.

  • LIFO queue: When the queue is full, drop the oldest requests (they have probably already timed out)
  • Priority-based: Shed low-priority traffic first (health checks, analytics) while preserving critical paths (checkout, auth)
  • Cooperative load shedding (CoDel): Google's algorithm that sheds requests based on queue wait time rather than queue depth

Staff-Level Signal

In interviews, connect these patterns together. A well-designed system uses timeouts + retries + circuit breakers + bulkheads as a defense-in-depth strategy. For example: "Calls to the payment service have a 3-second timeout, retry once with jitter on transient failures, trip a circuit breaker after 5 consecutive failures, and are isolated in a bulkhead with a max of 20 concurrent calls. If the circuit is open, we return a 'payment pending' response and process asynchronously via a message queue." This kind of layered thinking is what distinguishes staff engineers.

8. Capacity Planning

Load Testing and Scaling Strategies

Capacity planning ensures your system can handle expected and unexpected load. It combines load testing, trend analysis, and auto-scaling to prevent both over-provisioning (wasted money) and under-provisioning (outages).

Types of Performance Tests

Test TypePurposeDurationLoad Profile
Load test Verify behavior at expected peak 30-60 minutes Ramp up to 1x-2x expected peak
Stress test Find the breaking point 15-30 minutes Ramp beyond capacity until failure
Soak test (endurance) Detect memory leaks, resource exhaustion 4-24 hours Sustained moderate load
Spike test Verify auto-scaling response Variable Sudden 10x traffic spike

Load Testing Tools

k6 (Grafana)

JavaScript-based, developer-friendly. Excellent for CI/CD integration. Writes tests as code, supports complex scenarios, built-in metrics export to Grafana Cloud.

Locust (Python)

Python-based, distributed. Easy to define user behavior as Python code. Great web UI for real-time monitoring. Strong community.

Gatling (Scala/Java)

JVM-based, excellent for high throughput. Detailed HTML reports. Strong in enterprise environments. Good protocol support (HTTP, WebSocket, gRPC).

// k6 load test example (JavaScript)
import http from 'k6/http';
import { check, sleep } from 'k6';

export const options = {
  stages: [
    { duration: '2m', target: 100 },   // ramp up to 100 VUs
    { duration: '5m', target: 100 },   // hold at 100 VUs
    { duration: '2m', target: 500 },   // spike to 500 VUs
    { duration: '5m', target: 500 },   // hold at 500 VUs
    { duration: '2m', target: 0 },     // ramp down
  ],
  thresholds: {
    http_req_duration: ['p(99)<500'],  // p99 latency under 500ms
    http_req_failed: ['rate<0.01'],    // error rate under 1%
  },
};

export default function () {
  const res = http.get('https://api.example.com/products');
  check(res, {
    'status is 200': (r) => r.status === 200,
    'latency < 500ms': (r) => r.timings.duration < 500,
  });
  sleep(1); // simulate user think time
}

Auto-Scaling Strategies

  • Reactive auto-scaling: Scale based on current metrics (CPU > 70%, request queue depth > 100). Simple but always lagging behind demand.
  • Predictive auto-scaling: Use historical patterns (e.g., "traffic peaks at 9 AM EST on Mondays") to pre-scale before demand arrives. AWS and GCP both offer this.
  • Custom metrics scaling: Scale on application-specific metrics (active WebSocket connections, queue depth, GPU utilization) rather than generic CPU/memory.

Performance Budgets

Just as error budgets track reliability, performance budgets track latency and throughput ceilings. Define budgets for each service and break them down by component:

Checkout API total latency budget: 800ms (p99)
  ├── API Gateway + auth:     50ms
  ├── Order service logic:   100ms
  ├── Inventory DB query:    100ms
  ├── Payment service call:  400ms
  ├── Event publish (async):  50ms
  └── Margin / buffer:       100ms

If any component exceeds its budget, it requires investigation.

Staff-Level Signal

When discussing capacity planning, mention that load testing should happen regularly (weekly or on every major deployment), not just once before launch. Also discuss the difference between synthetic load tests and production load analysis. Synthetic tests may not capture realistic access patterns (zipfian distributions, hot keys). Staff engineers combine both: load tests for baseline + production profiling for real-world patterns.

9. Real-World Case Studies

Google SRE: Error Budgets in Practice

Google's SRE team has operated at massive scale for over a decade, and their error budget approach is now the gold standard. Key lessons:

  • Error budgets align incentives: Product teams and SRE teams used to be adversarial (product wants to ship, SRE wants stability). Error budgets gave them a shared vocabulary: "We have 15 minutes of budget left this month; let's postpone the risky migration until next month."
  • Not all services need the same SLO: Google Search has a much tighter SLO than an internal admin tool. Google Maps tiles (static data) have a different SLO profile than real-time traffic updates.
  • SLO reviews happen quarterly: Teams review whether their SLO is too tight (wasting engineering effort on reliability that users don't need) or too loose (accumulating tech debt).
  • Toil budgets complement error budgets: Google SRE defines "toil" as repetitive, manual work that could be automated. Teams target <50% of time spent on toil; the rest goes to engineering improvements.
AWS Outage Postmortems: Lessons Learned

AWS publishes detailed postmortems for major outages. Some of the most instructive:

US-EAST-1 S3 Outage (Feb 2017)

An engineer executing a routine playbook accidentally removed more S3 servers than intended. The S3 subsystem took over 4 hours to recover because the indexing and placement services had not been restarted in years and took much longer than expected to bootstrap.

Lessons:

  • Safeguards on operational commands should prevent removing too many servers at once
  • Regularly restart long-running subsystems so recovery paths are exercised
  • Blast radius: half the internet was affected because so many services depended on us-east-1 S3

US-EAST-1 Kinesis Outage (Nov 2020)

A small capacity addition to the Kinesis front-end fleet triggered a cascade of failures in the internal metadata service. The number of threads used for inter-server communication scaled with the number of servers, and adding new servers pushed the thread count past OS limits.

Lessons:

  • Capacity additions can be as risky as code deployments
  • Hidden coupling between fleet size and thread count was not tested
  • Cascading dependencies: CloudWatch, Lambda, Cognito, and many other services depended on Kinesis and were affected
Slack: Observability at Scale

Slack processes billions of events per day and has invested heavily in observability. Key aspects of their approach:

  • Custom metrics pipeline: Slack built Bedrock, a custom metrics aggregation system, because off-the-shelf tools could not handle their cardinality and throughput requirements (millions of unique time series).
  • Trace-driven debugging: When a user reports "Slack is slow," their support tooling can pull the specific trace for that user's session, showing exactly where latency was introduced.
  • Canary deployments with automatic rollback: Every deployment goes to a canary fleet first. If error rate or latency degrades compared to the control group, the deployment is automatically rolled back within minutes.
  • Disasterpiece Theater: Slack's regular chaos engineering exercises, where they simulate production failures and evaluate response. Teams write up the results and share learnings across the organization.
  • On-call health: Slack tracks on-call metrics (pages per shift, time to acknowledge, sleep interruptions) and uses this data to improve alert quality and reduce toil.

10. Interview Application

Integrating Monitoring into Every Design Answer

At staff level, monitoring and reliability are not optional add-ons -- they are expected components of every system design answer. Here is how to weave them in naturally:

The Monitoring Section in Your Answer

After discussing the core architecture, dedicate 3-5 minutes to observability:

  1. Define SLIs/SLOs: "For this URL shortener, our primary SLIs would be redirect latency (p99 < 50ms), availability (99.99%), and redirect success rate (>99.9%)."
  2. Identify key metrics: "I'd track redirects per second (traffic), cache hit rate (efficiency), expired link lookups (errors), and database connection pool utilization (saturation)."
  3. Describe alerting: "We'd alert on error rate exceeding 0.5% for 5 minutes (symptom-based) and cache hit rate dropping below 80% (cause-based, non-paging)."
  4. Discuss failure modes: "If the database is down, we serve from cache with a degraded experience. If cache is down, we fall through to the database with a circuit breaker."
  5. Mention tracing: "Each redirect would carry a trace ID so we can debug latency issues across the load balancer, cache, and database layers."

Common Interview Questions

"What metrics would you track for system X?"

Use the RED method for user-facing services and USE for infrastructure. Organize your answer around the four golden signals: latency, traffic, errors, saturation. Always mention business metrics alongside technical metrics (e.g., "orders per minute" not just "requests per second").

"How would you detect and handle failures?"

Structure your answer as: Detect (health checks, metric-based alerts, anomaly detection) -> Contain (circuit breakers, bulkheads, load shedding) -> Recover (automated failover, graceful degradation, rollback) -> Learn (postmortem, action items). This shows a mature, systematic approach.

"How would you ensure this system is reliable?"

Cover all layers: Prevention (testing, code review, canary deploys), Detection (monitoring, alerting, SLOs), Mitigation (circuit breakers, retries, graceful degradation), Recovery (automated rollback, failover, disaster recovery), and Learning (postmortems, chaos engineering). Mention specific nines of availability and justify them.

Mistakes to Avoid

  • Designing only the happy path and not discussing failure modes
  • Saying "we'll add monitoring" as an afterthought without specifics
  • Proposing 99.999% availability without discussing the extreme cost
  • Ignoring cascading failures in microservice architectures
  • No mention of how you would detect problems (alerting) vs. just logging
  • Proposing retries without discussing idempotency

Staff-Level Signal

The strongest signal you can send in a system design interview is proactively discussing failure modes without being asked. When you describe a component, immediately follow with: "This could fail in these ways, and here's how we handle each one." Staff engineers think about reliability as a first-class concern, not a bolt-on. This is the single biggest differentiator between senior and staff-level answers.
