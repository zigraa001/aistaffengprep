The RESHADE Framework Overview

RESHADE is a structured, seven-phase approach to system design interviews. It gives you a repeatable skeleton so you never freeze during the interview, while leaving room for the creative, judgment-driven work that distinguishes staff engineers. The name is a mnemonic: Requirements, Estimation, Storage Schema, High-Level Design, API Design, Deep Dive, Edge Cases.

5-8 min"] --> E["Estimation
3-5 min"] E --> S["Storage Schema
3-5 min"] S --> H["High-Level Design
10-15 min"] H --> A["API Design

15-20 min"] D --> E2["Edge Cases

Why a framework matters: Google's internal interviewer training found that candidates who use a structured approach score 30-40% higher on "organization and communication" rubrics than those who jump straight into drawing boxes. The framework is not a straitjacket — it is a scaffold. You can reorder phases (some interviewers prefer API before schema) or merge them, but you should always cover all seven areas.

Phase 1: Requirements (R) — 5-8 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

The requirements phase is where you transform a vague prompt ("Design Twitter") into a well-scoped problem statement. This is the most underrated phase — many candidates rush through it in under two minutes, then spend the next 40 minutes designing the wrong system. At the staff level, this phase is where you demonstrate product sense and the ability to scope ambiguity.

There are two categories of requirements you must elicit:

Functional Requirements (FRs)

These are the features the system must support. Your goal is to narrow the scope to 3-5 core features. A common mistake is trying to design everything — the interviewer wants depth, not breadth.

  • What are the core user actions? (create, read, update, delete)
  • Who are the users? (end users, internal services, third-party developers)
  • What are the key user journeys? (the critical path from input to output)
  • Are there different user roles or access levels?
  • What features are explicitly out of scope? (ask this directly)

Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs)

These constrain how the system behaves. Staff engineers are expected to drive this conversation proactively, not wait for the interviewer to volunteer numbers.

  • Scale: DAU, total users, data volume, growth rate
  • Latency: p50, p99 targets for critical paths
  • Availability: 99.9% (8.7h downtime/year) vs 99.99% (52min/year)
  • Consistency: Strong, eventual, or causal? Per-feature?
  • Durability: Can we ever lose data? What's the RPO?
  • Cost: Are we optimizing for cost or performance?

Time Allocation & Pacing

Spend 5-8 minutes here. If the interviewer seems eager to move on after 3 minutes, respect that — but make sure you have at least confirmed the core FRs, scale, and consistency model. You can always revisit requirements later ("Now that I'm thinking about the notification path, can I confirm whether we need exactly-once delivery?").

Worked Example: Design Twitter — Requirements Phase ▼

Candidate: "Before I start designing, I'd like to scope this properly. When you say 'Design Twitter,' I imagine the core features are: posting tweets, reading a home timeline (feed of tweets from people you follow), following/unfollowing users, and searching tweets. Are there any features you'd like me to prioritize or exclude?"

Interviewer: "Let's focus on posting tweets and the home timeline. You can skip search and DMs."

Candidate: "Great. Let me confirm a few non-functional requirements:"

  • "What scale are we targeting? I'll assume Twitter-scale: ~500M total users, ~200M DAU."
  • "Read-heavy: each user views the timeline many times per day but tweets infrequently. I'd estimate a 100:1 read-to-write ratio."
  • "For latency, timeline loads should be under 200ms at p99."
  • "For consistency, I think eventual consistency is acceptable for the timeline — a tweet appearing a few seconds late is fine — but the tweet itself must be durably stored once acknowledged. Does that sound right?"
  • "I'll assume global distribution across at least 3 regions."

Result: The candidate has scoped the problem to two core features, established concrete numbers for estimation, and demonstrated product sense by reasoning about the consistency model per feature.

Scoped FRs:
  1. Post a tweet (text, up to 280 chars, optionally with media)
  2. View home timeline (tweets from people you follow, reverse chronological with ranking)
  3. Follow/unfollow users
Scoped NFRs: 200M DAU, 100:1 read:write, <200ms p99 timeline latency, eventual consistency for feed, strong consistency for tweet writes, 99.99% availability, multi-region.
Common Mistakes in Requirements Phase ▼
  • Mistake: Skipping requirements entirely. Some candidates say "OK, so Twitter — let me draw the architecture." This is the single most common reason candidates fail. You end up designing features the interviewer did not care about.
  • Mistake: Asking too many questions without making assumptions. Don't ask 20 questions one by one. Instead, state your assumptions and ask the interviewer to correct you: "I'll assume 200M DAU — does that sound reasonable?" This is faster and shows confidence.
  • Mistake: Not scoping down. "Design Twitter" has hundreds of features. If you try to cover tweets, DMs, search, notifications, ads, trending topics, and Spaces in 45 minutes, you will get shallow coverage of everything and deep coverage of nothing.
  • Mistake: Ignoring non-functional requirements. Many candidates only ask about features. NFRs (scale, latency, consistency) are what drive architectural decisions. A system for 1,000 users is fundamentally different from one for 200M users.

Phase 2: Estimation (E) — 3-5 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

Estimation is not about getting exact numbers — it is about demonstrating that you can reason quantitatively and that your architectural choices are grounded in reality. The interviewer wants to see that you know the difference between a system handling 100 QPS and one handling 100K QPS, because they require fundamentally different architectures.

There are three types of estimation you should cover:

1. Traffic Estimation (QPS)

Start with DAU, multiply by actions-per-user, divide by seconds-per-day. Always calculate both average and peak QPS.

DAU: 200M users Tweets per user/day: 0.5 (most users read, not write) Write QPS: 200M x 0.5 / 86,400 = ~1,150 writes/sec Peak write QPS: ~3,500 writes/sec (3x average) Timeline reads/user/day: 50 (including refreshes) Read QPS: 200M x 50 / 86,400 = ~115,000 reads/sec Peak read QPS: ~350,000 reads/sec

2. Storage Estimation

Estimate per-record size, multiply by volume, project over time (typically 5 years).

Tweet size (average): - tweet_id: 8 bytes - user_id: 8 bytes - text: 280 bytes (worst case) - timestamp: 8 bytes - metadata: ~100 bytes (likes count, retweet count, etc.) - Total: ~400 bytes per tweet Tweets per day: 200M x 0.5 = 100M tweets/day Storage per day: 100M x 400B = 40 GB/day Storage per year: 40 GB x 365 = ~15 TB/year 5-year storage: ~75 TB (text only, excluding media)

3. Bandwidth Estimation

Often skipped, but valuable for CDN and network planning.

Read bandwidth: 350K reads/sec x 400B = ~140 MB/sec (text only) With media: If 20% of tweets have images (avg 200KB): 350K x 0.2 x 200KB = ~14 GB/sec = ~112 Gbps This is why you need a CDN!

Time Allocation & Pacing

Spend 3-5 minutes. Do the math out loud so the interviewer can follow. Round aggressively — 86,400 is "about 100K" and that is perfectly fine. If the interviewer says "let's skip estimation," don't insist, but mention that you've sized it mentally and will reference the numbers later.

Common Mistakes & Staff-Level Signals ▼

Common Mistakes

  • Spending too long on math. Estimation is a means to an end. If you spend 10 minutes on back-of-envelope calculations, you've burned time you need for the deep dive.
  • Not using the numbers later. If you calculate 350K reads/sec but then propose a single MySQL instance (which tops out at ~20K QPS), you've contradicted yourself. The numbers must inform your architecture.
  • Being too precise. "The QPS is 115,740.74" suggests you don't understand that these are order-of-magnitude estimates. Say "roughly 100K reads/sec."
  • Forgetting peak vs. average. Systems must handle peaks. A 3x peak factor is typical; events like the Super Bowl can cause 10x+ spikes.

Staff-Level Signals

  • Connects estimation to architecture: "At 350K reads/sec, a single database won't cut it. We'll need caching and read replicas."
  • Considers the long tail: "Most users have <1,000 followers, but some celebrities have 50M+. The average-case math looks fine, but we need a different strategy for those outliers."
  • Estimates cost: "At 75TB over 5 years, that's about $1,500/month on S3 for storage alone, plus transfer costs."

Template Phrases

"Let me do some quick math to size this system. I'll round aggressively to keep things moving." "So we're looking at roughly [X]K writes/sec and [Y]K reads/sec. That tells me we'll definitely need [caching/sharding/read replicas]." "Over 5 years, we're looking at about [X] TB of storage. That's manageable with [storage approach], but we should plan for tiered storage to manage costs."

Phase 3: Storage Schema (S) — 3-5 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

In this phase you define how data is stored. This is not just about drawing ER diagrams — it is about making deliberate choices between SQL and NoSQL, between normalized and denormalized schemas, between row-oriented and column-oriented storage, and being able to articulate why you made each choice based on the access patterns and scale you identified in the previous phases.

Key Decisions

  • SQL vs NoSQL: SQL (PostgreSQL, MySQL) when you need ACID transactions, complex joins, or strong consistency. NoSQL (Cassandra, DynamoDB, MongoDB) when you need horizontal scalability, flexible schemas, or access-pattern-driven design.
  • Partition Key: The single most important decision in a distributed database. Choose a key with high cardinality (many distinct values) and even distribution. Bad partition key = hot partitions = outages.
  • Normalization vs. Denormalization: Normalized schemas reduce redundancy but require joins. In distributed systems, joins across partitions are expensive or impossible, so denormalization is often preferred on the read path.
  • Indexing Strategy: Define access patterns first, then design indexes. Secondary indexes in distributed databases (Cassandra, DynamoDB) are not free — they create additional write amplification and consistency challenges.

Time Allocation & Pacing

Spend 3-5 minutes. Sketch your main tables/collections, their key columns, and the partition strategy. Don't enumerate every column — focus on the ones that drive architectural decisions (the partition key, sort key, and any columns you'll query by).

Worked Example: Design Twitter — Storage Schema ▼

Candidate: "For the storage layer, I'll use a combination of databases optimized for different access patterns."

Users Table (PostgreSQL)

users - user_id BIGINT PRIMARY KEY -- Snowflake ID - username VARCHAR(15) UNIQUE - display_name VARCHAR(50) - bio VARCHAR(160) - followers_count INT - following_count INT - created_at TIMESTAMP Why PostgreSQL: User data is relatively small (<500M rows), requires strong consistency (you can't have two users with the same username), and benefits from ACID for profile updates.

Tweets Table (Cassandra)

tweets - tweet_id BIGINT -- Snowflake ID (encodes timestamp) - user_id BIGINT -- Partition key - text VARCHAR(280) - media_urls LIST<TEXT> - likes_count COUNTER - retweet_count COUNTER - created_at TIMESTAMP -- Clustering key (DESC) PRIMARY KEY (user_id, created_at DESC) Why Cassandra: 100M tweets/day requires horizontal write scalability. Partition by user_id gives us fast "get all tweets by user" queries. Cassandra handles the write throughput (~3.5K/sec) easily across a modest cluster. The trade-off: no cross-user joins, but we don't need them for our access patterns.

Follow Graph (Cassandra or dedicated graph store)

follows - follower_id BIGINT -- Partition key - followee_id BIGINT -- Clustering key - created_at TIMESTAMP Reverse index: follows_reverse - followee_id BIGINT -- Partition key - follower_id BIGINT -- Clustering key Why two tables: We need both directions. "Who do I follow?" (for timeline generation) and "Who follows me?" (for follower count, fan-out). Cassandra doesn't support reverse lookups on clustering keys, so we maintain both and accept the write amplification.

Timeline Cache (Redis)

Key: timeline:{user_id} Value: Sorted set of tweet_ids, scored by timestamp TTL: 24 hours (cold timelines are regenerated on access) Why Redis: The timeline is a derived, read-optimized view. We pre-compute it via fan-out-on-write for most users and store the last 800 tweet IDs. Redis sorted sets give us O(log N) insertions and O(K) range queries.
Staff-Level Signal: Notice the candidate didn't just list tables — they explained why each storage technology was chosen, tied the partition key to access patterns, and acknowledged trade-offs (write amplification for the follow graph, eventual consistency for timelines).
Common Mistakes & Staff-Level Signals ▼

Common Mistakes

  • Picking a database without justification. "I'll use MongoDB" — why? What access patterns does it optimize for? How does it handle the scale you estimated?
  • Using a single database for everything. At Twitter scale, a single PostgreSQL instance cannot handle both tweet writes (100M/day) and timeline reads (10B/day). Polyglot persistence is the norm.
  • Ignoring the partition key. In distributed databases, the partition key determines data distribution, query performance, and hot-spot risk. Not discussing it is a red flag.
  • Over-engineering the schema. Listing 20 columns with precise data types wastes time. Focus on the columns that matter for partitioning, querying, and indexing.

Template Phrases

"I'll use [database] for [entity] because our access pattern is [description], and [database] optimizes for that." "I'm partitioning by [key] because it gives us high cardinality and even distribution. The risk is [hot partition scenario], which I'll address in the deep dive." "I'm denormalizing [field] into [table] to avoid a cross-partition join on the read path. The trade-off is write amplification and the need for eventual consistency between the source and the copy."

Phase 4: High-Level Design (H) — 10-15 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

This is the "draw the architecture" phase. You're assembling the major components into a coherent system. The goal is a diagram that shows how data flows from the client through your system and back, covering both the write path and the read path.

At the staff level, you should be thinking about:

  • Separation of concerns: Each box in your diagram should have one clear responsibility.
  • Sync vs. async paths: Which operations happen in the request-response cycle, and which are offloaded to background workers via queues?
  • Data flow: Trace a single request from client to storage and back. Where does data get cached? Where does it get transformed?
  • Failure boundaries: If this component dies, what happens? A well-designed system degrades gracefully rather than cascading.

These are the building blocks you'll use in virtually every system design:

Spend 10-15 minutes. Draw the diagram incrementally: start with the client and API layer, then add storage, then add caching and async paths. Narrate as you draw — never silently draw for 5 minutes.

Candidate narration: "Let me walk through the two critical paths."

Write Path (Posting a Tweet)

  1. Client sends POST request to the Tweet Service via the load balancer.
  2. Tweet Service validates the request, generates a Snowflake ID, writes the tweet to Cassandra.
  3. If there's media, it's uploaded directly to S3 (via a pre-signed URL to avoid proxying through our servers).
  4. Tweet Service publishes a message to the Kafka fan-out topic.
  5. Fan-out workers consume the message, look up the poster's followers, and insert the tweet ID into each follower's timeline in Redis.
  6. For celebrities (>1M followers), we skip fan-out-on-write and instead merge their tweets at read time (fan-out-on-read hybrid).

Read Path (Loading the Timeline)

  1. Client sends GET request to the Timeline Service.
  2. Timeline Service reads the user's pre-computed timeline from Redis (list of tweet IDs).
  3. For celebrity tweets: merges the user's followed celebrities' recent tweets at read time.
  4. Hydrates tweet IDs by fetching full tweet data from Cassandra (with a local cache for popular tweets).
  5. Returns the assembled timeline to the client. Media URLs point to the CDN.
Staff-Level Signal: The hybrid fan-out approach (fan-out-on-write for normal users, fan-out-on-read for celebrities) is exactly how Twitter actually works. This shows the candidate understands the trade-off: fan-out-on-write is fast to read but expensive for users with millions of followers; fan-out-on-read is cheap to write but adds read latency.
Common Mistakes & Staff-Level Signals ▼

Common Mistakes

  • Drawing a monolith. A single "API Server" box connecting to a single "Database" box is not a high-level design. Break your system into services with clear boundaries.
  • Not showing data flow. Boxes without arrows are meaningless. Every arrow should represent a specific data flow (request/response, event, async message).
  • Missing the async path. Almost every real system has background processing. If your design is purely synchronous, you're missing a critical dimension.
  • Not explaining the "why." Drawing a message queue is easy. Explaining why you need it (decoupling the write acknowledgment from the fan-out, smoothing traffic spikes, enabling retry) is what matters.

Template Phrases

"Let me trace the write path first, then the read path." "I'm introducing a message queue here to decouple [X] from [Y]. This means we can acknowledge the write to the user immediately while the fan-out happens asynchronously." "I'm splitting this into separate services because [Tweet Service] and [Timeline Service] have very different scaling characteristics: tweets are write-heavy while timelines are read-heavy."

Phase 5: API Design (A) — 3-5 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

API design defines the contract between clients and your system. It is the system's external promise. At the staff level, you should think about APIs not just as endpoint signatures, but as a product surface that impacts developer experience, client performance, and long-term maintainability.

Key Considerations

  • REST vs. gRPC vs. GraphQL: REST for public APIs (well-understood, cacheable). gRPC for internal service-to-service communication (efficient, strongly typed). GraphQL when clients need flexible queries (mobile apps with varying data needs).
  • Pagination: Cursor-based pagination (using an opaque token) is almost always better than offset-based for large datasets. Offset-based breaks when data is inserted or deleted between pages.
  • Idempotency: For non-GET requests, include an idempotency key so retries don't create duplicate resources. This is critical for payment systems but good practice everywhere.
  • Rate Limiting: Include rate limit headers (X-RateLimit-Limit, X-RateLimit-Remaining) so clients can self-throttle.
  • Versioning: URL path versioning (/v1/tweets) is most common. Header-based versioning is more RESTful but harder to debug.
  • Backward Compatibility: Adding fields is safe. Removing or renaming fields is a breaking change. Plan for API evolution from day one.

Time Allocation & Pacing

Spend 3-5 minutes. Define 3-5 core endpoints. You don't need to exhaustively list every field — focus on the endpoints that drive the system's critical paths and mention any non-obvious design decisions (cursor pagination, idempotency, etc.).

Staff-Level Signals in API Design:
  • Mentioning idempotency keys for write operations
  • Using cursor-based pagination instead of offset
  • Pre-signed URLs for media upload (avoids server bottleneck)
  • Including Cache-Control headers in the response
  • Separating media upload from tweet creation (allows parallel upload)

Phase 6: Deep Dive (D) — 15-20 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

This is the most important phase and where staff-level candidates differentiate themselves. The interviewer will pick 1-2 areas of your design and drill deep. Alternatively — and this is a strong staff signal — you proactively identify the hardest problem and dive in.

The deep dive tests whether you can go beyond "boxes and arrows" into the implementation details that make or break a system in production. Common deep-dive topics include:

  • Scaling bottlenecks: Which component breaks first at 10x scale? What's the mitigation? (shard, cache, queue, redesign)
  • Consistency model: Walk through a specific write and read flow. What happens during a network partition? What anomalies can a user see?
  • Data pipelines: How does data flow from write to being queryable? What's the end-to-end latency? Where can data be lost?
  • Hot spots: What happens when a celebrity with 50M followers tweets? How do you avoid thundering herd?
  • Operational concerns: How do you deploy safely (canary, blue-green)? What metrics and alerts would you set up? How do you debug a latency spike?
  • Migration strategy: How do you evolve the system from v1 to v2 without downtime?

Time Allocation & Pacing

This phase gets 15-20 minutes — the largest single block. Typically you'll cover 2-3 deep-dive topics. If the interviewer asks about one area, go deep (3-5 minutes per topic). If they give you freedom, proactively pick the hardest problem and say: "I think the trickiest part of this design is X — let me walk through how I'd handle it."

Worked Example: Design Twitter — Deep Dive on Fan-Out ▼

Candidate: "I think the hardest part of this design is the fan-out mechanism for the timeline. Let me go deep on that."

The Fan-Out Problem

When a user with N followers posts a tweet, we need to deliver it to N timelines. The naive approach (fan-out-on-write) works for most users but breaks for celebrities:

Normal user (1,000 followers): Fan-out cost: 1,000 Redis ZADD operations Time: ~10ms (pipelined) Totally fine. Celebrity (50M followers): Fan-out cost: 50,000,000 Redis ZADD operations Time: ~500 seconds (!) Unacceptable. Plus it creates a massive write spike.

The Hybrid Approach (How Twitter Actually Solved This)

flowchart TB Tweet[New Tweet] --> Check{Followers above threshold?} Check -->|No: under 10K followers| FOW[Fan-Out on Write] Check -->|Yes: celebrity| FOR[Fan-Out on Read] FOW --> Workers[Fan-out Workers] Workers --> Redis1[Push to each follower's
timeline in Redis] FOR --> Tag[Tag tweet as celebrity tweet
Store in celebrity timeline index] subgraph "Read Path" ReadReq[Timeline Request] --> Merge[Merge] Redis2[Users Redis Timeline] --> Merge CelebIdx[Celebrity Timeline Index
for followed celebs] --> Merge Merge --> Sort[Sort by timestamp] --> Return[Return top N] end

Implementation Details

  • Threshold: Twitter uses ~10,000 followers as the cutoff. Below this, fan-out-on-write. Above, fan-out-on-read. The threshold is tunable.
  • Fan-out workers: A pool of workers consumes from Kafka. Each worker handles a batch of followers. Backpressure is managed by Kafka consumer group lag monitoring.
  • Read-time merge: When loading a timeline, the service reads the pre-computed timeline from Redis AND the recent tweets from each celebrity the user follows. It merges and sorts these in-memory. Since a user follows maybe 10-20 celebrities, this is a small merge.
  • Consistency trade-off: Fan-out-on-write means a tweet appears in follower timelines within seconds (the time for the workers to process). Fan-out-on-read has zero delay since it's fetched live. The hybrid gives us the best of both worlds.

What if fan-out workers fall behind?

Candidate: "If Kafka consumer lag grows (meaning tweets are being produced faster than workers can fan them out), I'd handle it in three ways:"

  1. Auto-scaling: The worker fleet auto-scales based on consumer lag. More lag = more workers.
  2. Priority queues: Tweets from users whose followers are currently online get higher priority. No point fanning out to someone who won't check Twitter for 6 hours.
  3. Graceful degradation: If lag exceeds a threshold (say 5 minutes), temporarily switch more users to fan-out-on-read. The timeline service already supports both paths, so this is just a configuration change.
Why this deep dive scores well: The candidate identified the hardest problem without being asked, explained the trade-offs quantitatively, described the real-world solution, and discussed operational concerns (monitoring lag, auto-scaling, graceful degradation). This is what staff-level looks like.
Common Mistakes & Staff-Level Signals ▼

Common Mistakes

  • Staying too high-level. "We'd use a cache" is not a deep dive. What cache? What eviction policy? What's the cache hit rate? What happens on a cache miss? What about cache stampede?
  • Not identifying the hard problem. Every system has a "crux" — the one thing that, if you get wrong, the whole design falls apart. For Twitter, it's fan-out. For a chat system, it's real-time message delivery. For a rate limiter, it's distributed counting. If you don't identify and address the crux, the interviewer will do it for you, and that costs you points.
  • Hand-waving on failure modes. "If the cache goes down, we fall back to the database" — but at 350K reads/sec, will the database survive? What's the latency impact? How long until the cache is warm again?
  • Not being quantitative. "This will be fast" is not a staff-level statement. "This merge operation combines at most 20 sorted lists of 50 items each, which is O(1000 log 20) — well under 1ms" is.

Template Phrases

"I think the trickiest part of this design is [X]. Let me walk through how I'd handle it." "There's a fundamental tension here between [A] and [B]. Let me explain the trade-off and why I'm choosing [A]." "Let me trace through a specific scenario: what happens when [edge case]. At step 3, we have a problem because [issue]. My solution is [fix]." "This works for the common case, but there's a pathological scenario: [description]. To handle it, I'd add [mitigation]."

Phase 7: Edge Cases & Failure Modes (E) — 5-10 Minutes

Detailed Explanation ▼

The edge cases phase demonstrates operational maturity. You're showing the interviewer that you don't just design systems for the happy path — you design them for the messy reality of production. This phase covers failure modes, security, cost, and the long-term evolution of the system.

Categories of Edge Cases

  • Infrastructure failures: Database goes down, cache goes cold, network partition between data centers, disk fills up, DNS fails.
  • Traffic anomalies: Celebrity tweet goes viral (thundering herd), DDoS attack, flash sale, bot traffic.
  • Data integrity: Partial failures (payment charged but order not created), duplicate messages, out-of-order events, clock skew.
  • Operational concerns: Schema migration on a live database, rolling back a bad deployment, data corruption recovery, debugging latency in production.
  • Security: Input validation, SQL injection, XSS, rate limiting abuse, data exfiltration, GDPR compliance (right to delete).
  • Cost at scale: Compute, storage, bandwidth, and third-party API costs. Tiered storage, reserved instances, spot instances.

Time Allocation & Pacing

Spend 5-10 minutes. You won't cover everything — pick the 3-4 most impactful edge cases for your specific design. Prioritize failure modes that could cause data loss or prolonged outages.

Worked Example: Design Twitter — Edge Cases ▼

Candidate: "Let me cover the key failure modes and edge cases for this design."

1. Cache Failure (Redis Cluster Down)

"If the Redis timeline cache goes down, we cannot serve timelines at our target latency from Cassandra alone — 350K reads/sec would overwhelm the database. My mitigation:"

  • Redis cluster with replication (3 replicas per shard). A single node failure doesn't affect availability.
  • If the entire cluster fails, circuit-break the timeline endpoint and serve a degraded experience: show the user's own recent tweets and trending tweets (from a separate, independent cache).
  • On recovery, warm the cache lazily: only rebuild a user's timeline when they request it, not all at once.

2. Thundering Herd (Celebrity Tweet)

"When a celebrity with 50M followers tweets, millions of users may refresh their timeline simultaneously, all requesting the same tweet. Without mitigation, this creates a read amplification storm."

  • Celebrity tweets are cached at the CDN edge (short TTL: 5-10 seconds).
  • The Timeline Service uses request coalescing: if 1,000 concurrent requests ask for the same celebrity tweet, only one goes to Cassandra. The others wait for and share the result.
  • Pre-warm: when the fan-out system detects a celebrity tweet, it proactively pushes the tweet data to the local caches of all Timeline Service instances.

3. Data Integrity: Duplicate Tweets

"Network retries can cause the same tweet to be written twice. We handle this with the idempotency key: the Tweet Service checks a short-TTL cache (or DynamoDB table) of recent idempotency keys before creating a new tweet. If the key already exists, we return the existing tweet instead of creating a duplicate."

4. GDPR Right to Delete

"When a user requests account deletion, we need to remove their data from the tweets table, the fan-out timelines, the follow graph, and any analytics pipelines. This is a background job that can take hours due to the volume of data. We mark the account as 'pending deletion' immediately (soft delete) and run the hard delete asynchronously, generating an audit log for compliance."

5. Cost Optimization

"At 15TB/year of tweet storage, after 5 years we have 75TB. Tweets older than 30 days are rarely accessed. I'd implement tiered storage: hot tweets (<30 days) in Cassandra with SSDs, warm tweets (30 days - 1 year) in Cassandra with HDDs, cold tweets (>1 year) in S3 with a metadata index for retrieval."

Common Mistakes & Staff-Level Signals ▼

Common Mistakes

  • Only discussing the happy path. If everything goes right, any design works. The interviewer wants to know what happens when things go wrong.
  • Generic failure handling. "We'd have retries and fallbacks" is too vague. What do you retry? How many times? With what backoff? What's the fallback? What does the user see?
  • Ignoring cost. At staff level, you're expected to think about the business impact of your design. Infrastructure costs matter.

Template Phrases

"Let me walk through the failure modes. The most impactful failure in this system is [X] because [reason]." "If [component] goes down, here's the blast radius: [affected features]. My mitigation is [strategy], which limits the impact to [scope]." "For data integrity, I'm concerned about [scenario]. I'd prevent this with [mechanism]: specifically, [implementation detail]." "From a cost perspective, the biggest line item will be [X]. To optimize, I'd implement [strategy] which reduces cost by approximately [Y]%."

Putting It All Together: Full 45-Minute Mock Walkthrough

Design a Notification System

Scenario: You are interviewing for a Staff Engineer position. The interviewer says: "Design a notification system that can send push notifications, emails, and SMS to hundreds of millions of users." Below is a complete walkthrough showing exactly how to structure your 45 minutes.
Minutes 0-6: Requirements (R) ▼

Candidate: "Great problem. Let me clarify the requirements before diving in."

Functional Requirements (confirmed with interviewer)

  1. Send notifications via three channels: push (iOS/Android), email, SMS
  2. User preferences: Users can opt in/out of channels and notification types
  3. Template system: Notifications use templates with variable substitution
  4. Scheduling: Notifications can be sent immediately or scheduled for a future time
  5. Rate limiting: Don't spam users (max N notifications per channel per hour)

De-scoped: Analytics dashboard, A/B testing, in-app notification center.

Non-Functional Requirements

  • Scale: 500M registered users, ~100M notifications/day (across all channels)
  • Latency: Real-time notifications delivered within 5 seconds of trigger. Scheduled within 1 minute of target time.
  • Reliability: At-least-once delivery. No notification silently lost. Duplicates are tolerable (better to get 2 than 0).
  • Availability: 99.95% for the ingestion API. The delivery pipeline can briefly buffer during outages.

Cross-Cutting Concerns

The following components are accessed by multiple services in the pipeline:

  • Rate Limiter (Redis): Checked during validation to prevent spamming users
  • User Preferences (PostgreSQL + Cache): Checked during validation to respect user opt-outs and quiet hours
  • Template Engine: Used by channel-specific workers to render notification content

Write Path (Sending a Notification)

  1. Trigger: An internal service (e.g., Orders Service) calls the Notification API: "Send a 'order shipped' notification to user 12345."
  2. Validation & Enrichment: The ingestion service checks user preferences (does this user want 'order' notifications?), applies rate limiting (have we exceeded the hourly cap?), and resolves the template.
  3. Routing: If the notification is immediate, it goes to Kafka. If scheduled, it goes to the schedule store.
  4. Channel-specific queues: A priority router consumes from the main Kafka topic and routes to channel-specific queues. This allows independent scaling: push workers can scale separately from email workers.
  5. Delivery: Channel-specific workers render the template, call the third-party provider (FCM, SES, Twilio), and log the result.
  6. Status tracking: Every state transition (pending, sent, delivered, failed) is written to the notification log in Cassandra.

Minutes 28-42: Deep Dive (D) ▼

Candidate: "I'd like to deep-dive on two areas: reliability (ensuring no notification is lost) and the rate limiting system."

Deep Dive 1: At-Least-Once Delivery Guarantee

"The biggest risk in a notification system is silently dropping messages. Here's how I ensure at-least-once delivery end-to-end:"

  1. Kafka with acks=all: The producer waits for all in-sync replicas to acknowledge. This means a message is not lost even if a broker dies.
  2. Consumer offset management: Workers commit Kafka offsets after successfully calling the third-party API and writing to the notification log, not before. If a worker crashes mid-processing, the message is redelivered.
  3. Dead letter queue (DLQ): If delivery fails after 3 retries (with exponential backoff), the message goes to a DLQ for manual investigation. The DLQ is monitored with alerts.
  4. Idempotency at the provider level: Push and email providers handle duplicates gracefully. For SMS (where duplicates cost money), we check the notification log before sending: if the notification_id already has a "sent" status, skip it.
  5. Reconciliation job: A periodic job (every 15 minutes) scans the notification log for notifications stuck in "pending" status for more than 10 minutes and re-enqueues them. This catches any edge case where Kafka delivery or worker processing silently failed.

Deep Dive 2: Distributed Rate Limiting

"We need to enforce per-user, per-channel rate limits across a distributed fleet of workers. A naive approach using a local counter won't work because requests for the same user can hit different workers."

# Redis sliding window rate limiter # Key: rate_limit:{user_id}:{channel} # Using sorted set with timestamp as score def check_rate_limit(user_id, channel, max_per_hour): key = f"rate_limit:{user_id}:{channel}" now = time.time() window_start = now - 3600 # 1 hour ago pipe = redis.pipeline() pipe.zremrangebyscore(key, 0, window_start) # Remove old entries pipe.zcard(key) # Count current entries pipe.zadd(key, {str(now): now}) # Add current request pipe.expire(key, 3600) # Set TTL results = pipe.execute() current_count = results[1] if current_count >= max_per_hour: # Remove the entry we just added redis.zrem(key, str(now)) return False # Rate limited return True # Allowed

"This uses Redis sorted sets for a sliding window. The pipeline ensures atomicity. The TTL ensures we don't leak memory for inactive users. At 3,500 notifications/sec, Redis handles this easily (<0.1ms per check). If Redis is temporarily down, we fail open (allow the notification) rather than fail closed — it's better to slightly over-send than to silently drop."

Minutes 42-45: Edge Cases (E) ▼

Candidate: "Let me cover the critical edge cases."

  • Marketing blast (10M emails in 1 hour): "I'd queue the blast as a batch job that drip-feeds into the email queue at a controlled rate. SES has sending limits; we'd pre-negotiate higher limits and use a token bucket to stay under the provider's rate."
  • Invalid device tokens: "APNs returns an error for invalid tokens. We consume this feedback and remove stale tokens from Redis. Without this, we waste resources sending to dead endpoints and our push delivery rate drops over time."
  • Quiet hours: "If a notification arrives during the user's quiet hours, we don't drop it — we re-schedule it for 7:01 AM in their local timezone. This requires storing the user's timezone and doing a time check in the validation layer."
  • Provider outage (e.g., Twilio down): "The SMS workers detect failures via circuit breaker. When the circuit opens, messages accumulate in the SMS queue (Kafka retains them). When the provider recovers, messages drain naturally. We alert the ops team if the backlog exceeds 30 minutes."

Common Interviewer Follow-Ups

Interviewers use follow-up questions to probe the depth of your understanding. Here are 18 common follow-ups organized by category, with guidance on how to handle each one.

Scaling Follow-Ups (4 questions) ▼

1. "What happens if traffic grows 10x?"

How to handle: Identify the first bottleneck (usually the database or a single point of contention). Walk through your scaling strategy: horizontal scaling of stateless services, sharding the database, adding cache layers, using CDN. Be specific about which component breaks first and why.

2. "How would you shard the database?"

How to handle: Discuss partition key selection (high cardinality, even distribution, aligned with access patterns). Mention the risks: cross-shard queries, hot shards, rebalancing. Reference real-world examples (Instagram shards by user_id, Slack shards by workspace_id).

3. "What if one shard gets much more traffic than others?"

How to handle: This is the hot partition problem. Solutions include: consistent hashing with virtual nodes, splitting hot shards, caching hot data in front of the shard, or using a different partition key for the hot entity type.

4. "How would you handle a global user base across multiple regions?"

How to handle: Discuss multi-region architecture: data replication strategy (active-active vs active-passive), conflict resolution for concurrent writes, geo-routing of requests, and the latency trade-offs of cross-region replication. Mention CRDTs or last-writer-wins depending on the data type.

Reliability Follow-Ups (4 questions) ▼

5. "What happens when [component X] goes down?"

How to handle: For each critical component, have a clear answer: What's the blast radius? What does the user experience? What's the fallback? How long to recover? For example: "If the cache goes down, reads fall through to the database. At our traffic level, the database can handle 30% of normal read traffic, so we'd enable rate limiting and serve degraded results until the cache is rebuilt."

6. "How do you ensure exactly-once processing?"

How to handle: Be honest: true exactly-once is extremely hard in distributed systems. The practical approach is at-least-once delivery combined with idempotent consumers. Explain how you'd make consumers idempotent (deduplication table, idempotency keys, upserts instead of inserts).

7. "What if there's a network partition between your data centers?"

How to handle: This is a CAP theorem question. State your choice: if you chose availability (AP), explain what consistency anomalies users might see and how you resolve them. If you chose consistency (CP), explain the availability impact and which requests would fail.

8. "How do you handle cascading failures?"

How to handle: Discuss circuit breakers (Hystrix pattern), bulkheads (isolating failure domains), timeouts with retries (exponential backoff + jitter), load shedding, and graceful degradation. Give a concrete example: "If the recommendation service is slow, we return a pre-computed default set rather than blocking the page load."

Data & Consistency Follow-Ups (3 questions) ▼

9. "How do you keep the cache consistent with the database?"

How to handle: Discuss cache invalidation strategies: write-through (update cache on every write), write-behind (async batch updates), cache-aside (read-through with TTL), and event-driven invalidation (CDC from the database). Each has trade-offs. Write-through adds write latency; cache-aside can serve stale data for TTL duration; event-driven adds infrastructure complexity.

10. "What if two users update the same data simultaneously?"

How to handle: Discuss concurrency control: optimistic locking (version numbers / ETags), pessimistic locking (database locks), CRDTs (for specific data types), or operational transformation. The right choice depends on the conflict frequency and the cost of conflict resolution.

11. "How would you migrate from the old schema to the new one without downtime?"

How to handle: Describe the expand-contract pattern: (1) deploy code that writes to both old and new schemas, (2) backfill old data into the new schema, (3) switch reads to the new schema, (4) stop writing to the old schema, (5) drop the old schema. This is how Facebook, Google, and Stripe handle schema migrations at scale.

Operational & Security Follow-Ups (7 questions) ▼

12. "How would you monitor this system?"

How to handle: Discuss the four golden signals (latency, traffic, errors, saturation). Mention specific metrics for your design. Describe dashboards, alerts, and on-call escalation. Example: "I'd monitor Kafka consumer lag as a leading indicator. If lag exceeds 5 minutes, that's a P1 alert because notifications are being delayed."

13. "How would you deploy changes safely?"

How to handle: Discuss canary deployments (route 1-5% of traffic to the new version, monitor error rates, then gradually increase), feature flags (deploy code dark, then enable for a subset of users), and rollback strategy (automated rollback on error rate spike). Mention that stateless services are easy to roll back but database migrations are not.

14. "How do you handle security concerns?"

How to handle: Discuss authentication (OAuth2, JWT), authorization (RBAC or ABAC), input validation, encryption (TLS in transit, AES at rest), secrets management (HashiCorp Vault), and audit logging. For the specific system, mention domain-specific threats (e.g., for a notification system: preventing unauthorized notification sends, protecting PII in templates).

15. "How would you debug a latency spike in production?"

How to handle: Walk through a systematic approach: (1) Check dashboards for which service/endpoint is affected, (2) Look at distributed traces (Jaeger/Zipkin) to identify the slow hop, (3) Check resource utilization (CPU, memory, disk I/O, network), (4) Check for correlated events (deployment, traffic spike, GC pauses), (5) Reproduce and fix.

16. "What about GDPR / data privacy?"

How to handle: Discuss data classification (PII vs non-PII), right to deletion (soft delete + async hard delete), data retention policies, encryption of PII at rest, audit logs for data access, and data processing agreements with third-party providers.

17. "How do you handle testing at this scale?"

How to handle: Discuss unit tests, integration tests, load testing (with realistic traffic patterns), chaos engineering (Netflix's Chaos Monkey), shadow traffic (replay production traffic against a staging environment), and canary analysis.

18. "What would v2 of this system look like?"

How to handle: Discuss the system's evolution: what requirements would change? What tech debt would you pay down? What new capabilities would you add? Show that you think beyond the initial design. Example: "In v2, I'd add a real-time analytics pipeline for notification delivery metrics, an A/B testing framework for notification content, and replace the polling-based scheduler with a true distributed cron (like Airflow or Temporal)."

Pacing Tips

  • Use a mental clock. Glance at the time after each phase. If you've spent 12 minutes on requirements, you've over-invested.
  • Signpost transitions. Say "OK, I've covered requirements. Let me move to estimation." This helps both you and the interviewer track progress.
  • Budget 60% of time for HLD + Deep Dive. These are the highest-signal phases. If you have to cut something short, trim estimation or API design, not the deep dive.
  • Adapt to the interviewer. If the interviewer interrupts with questions, that's fine — they're steering you toward what they care about. Adjust your time allocation accordingly.
  • If you're running out of time: Say "I notice we have about 5 minutes left. Let me quickly cover the key edge cases and failure modes." This shows awareness and professionalism.

Communication Anti-Patterns (with fixes) ▼

Anti-Pattern: "I don't know"

Fix: It's OK to not know something. But instead of stopping, reason through it: "I haven't worked with Cassandra's lightweight transactions before, but based on my understanding of Paxos, I'd expect it to add latency due to the consensus round. Let me reason about whether we need that guarantee here."

Anti-Pattern: Defensive when challenged

Fix: When the interviewer pushes back, they're testing how you handle disagreement — a critical staff skill. Say: "That's a good point. Let me reconsider. If [their concern] is likely, then [alternative approach] might be better because [reason]."

Anti-Pattern: Not asking for feedback

Fix: At natural transitions, ask: "I'm about to move into the high-level design. Is there anything in the requirements or estimation you'd like me to revisit?" This shows you value the interviewer's input and treat the interview as a collaboration.

Anti-Pattern: Rigid adherence to framework order

Fix: RESHADE is a guide, not a law. If the interviewer asks about API design before you've drawn the HLD, go with it. Flexibility shows you can adapt to the conversation rather than mechanically following a script.
