Module 13: Scenario-Based System Thinking — Part 1

What You'll Learn

  • The scenario thinking framework: Understand → Clarify → Propose → Tradeoffs → Validate
  • Full walkthrough scenarios for webhook platforms, event-driven migrations, and payment testing
  • How to structure interview answers for scenario questions
  • Common mistakes and clarifying questions to always ask

The Framework

Q: What framework should you use for scenario-based interview questions?
graph LR U[1. Understand - Restate the problem] --> C[2. Clarify - Ask constraints] C --> P[3. Propose - Present approach] P --> T[4. Tradeoffs - Discuss alternatives] T --> V[5. Validate - Check with interviewer]
  1. Understand (1 min): Restate the problem in your own words to confirm you got it right
  2. Clarify (2-3 min): Ask about scale, constraints, existing systems, team size, timeline
  3. Propose (10-15 min): Walk through your approach step by step with diagrams
  4. Tradeoffs (5 min): Discuss alternatives you considered and why you chose this approach
  5. Validate (2 min): Summarize, check if the approach meets requirements, ask if anything was missed

Key principle: Think aloud. Interviewers evaluate your thought process, not just the final answer. Explaining why you rejected an approach is as valuable as explaining why you chose one.

Q: What clarifying questions should you always ask in a platform engineering scenario?
  • Scale: How many services/teams will use this? What's the expected throughput?
  • Existing systems: What's already in place? What can we build on vs replace?
  • Constraints: Budget? Timeline? Team size? Technology restrictions?
  • Requirements: What does "success" look like? SLOs? Adoption targets?
  • Users: Who are the consumers? What's their technical level?
  • Non-functional: Latency requirements? Compliance? Multi-region?

Why this matters: Asking clarifying questions shows seniority. Junior engineers jump straight to solutions. Staff engineers define the problem space first. The questions you ask tell the interviewer more about your experience than the answers you give.

Scenario Walkthroughs

Scenario 1: Design a webhook integration platform for a multi-tenant SaaS

Problem: Your company's SaaS platform has 500 customers who want to receive real-time event notifications when things happen in their account (order created, payment received, etc.). Design the webhook delivery system.

Clarifying questions to ask:

  • How many event types? (~20-50 event types)
  • Expected volume? (~10K events/minute across all tenants)
  • Delivery SLA? (Events delivered within 30 seconds)
  • Self-service or manual registration? (Self-service portal)

Proposed architecture:

graph LR SVC[Services] -->|Publish events| KAFKA[Kafka - event-bus topic] KAFKA --> ROUTER[Webhook Router] ROUTER --> |Lookup subscriptions| DB[(Subscription DB)] ROUTER --> DQ[Delivery Queue per tenant] DQ --> WORKER[Delivery Workers] WORKER --> |POST with HMAC| EP[Customer Endpoints] WORKER --> |Failure| RETRY[Retry Queue - exp backoff] RETRY --> WORKER RETRY --> |Max retries exceeded| DLQ[Dead Letter Queue]

Key design decisions:

  • Per-tenant delivery queues: A slow tenant endpoint shouldn't block other tenants' deliveries (bulkhead pattern)
  • HMAC signatures: Every webhook includes a signature so customers can verify authenticity
  • Idempotency: Include event ID so customers can deduplicate on their side
  • Retry policy: Exponential backoff: 1m, 5m, 30m, 2h, 8h, 24h. After 3 days, disable endpoint and notify customer.
  • Self-service portal: Customers register endpoints, select event types, view delivery logs, replay events

Tradeoffs discussed:

  • Kafka vs SQS: Kafka chosen for replay capability and ordering. SQS would be simpler but no replay.
  • Per-tenant queues vs shared queue: Per-tenant for isolation, but adds operational complexity.
  • Synchronous vs async delivery: Async with queues because synchronous delivery would make service latency dependent on customer endpoint speed.
Scenario 2: Migrate a REST API to event-driven architecture

Problem: Your order service currently uses synchronous REST calls to notify 5 downstream services (billing, shipping, inventory, notifications, analytics). Latency is growing and the coupling is becoming problematic. Design the migration to event-driven.

Current state (problematic):

graph LR OS[Order Service] -->|REST| BS[Billing] OS -->|REST| SS[Shipping] OS -->|REST| IS[Inventory] OS -->|REST| NS[Notifications] OS -->|REST| AS[Analytics]

Proposed migration (phased):

Phase 1 - Strangler Fig (weeks 1-4):

  • Add Kafka topic order-events
  • Order service publishes events AND continues REST calls (dual-write with outbox pattern)
  • Migrate analytics (lowest risk) to consume from Kafka. Verify parity. Remove REST call to analytics.

Phase 2 - Progressive migration (weeks 5-12):

  • Migrate notifications (low risk, idempotent)
  • Migrate inventory (medium risk, needs idempotency)
  • Migrate shipping (higher risk, involves external carriers)

Phase 3 - Complete migration (weeks 13-16):

  • Migrate billing (highest risk, involves money)
  • Remove all REST calls from order service
  • Remove dual-write code

Key tradeoffs: Eventual consistency (billing might be notified 100ms later), schema management (need schema registry), debugging complexity (harder to trace event flows than REST calls), but gained: decoupling, independent scaling, resilience (inventory down doesn't block orders).

Scenario 3: Design a testing strategy for a fintech payment platform

Problem: Your payment platform processes $10M/day. Design the testing strategy. An untested bug could cause double-charges, missed payments, or compliance violations.

Proposed testing portfolio:

LayerFocusToolsCoverage Target
Unit (30%)Payment calculation logic, fee computation, currency conversionpytest + Hypothesis (property-based)95% on core payment logic
Integration (40%)DB transactions, idempotency, Stripe/payment gateway interactionTestcontainers + WireMockAll payment flows exercised
Contract (10%)API compatibility with consumer servicesPactAll API boundaries
E2E (10%)Happy path: create → authorize → capture → settlePlaywright against staging5 critical journeys
Chaos (5%)Payment gateway timeout, DB failover, double-submissionToxiproxy + custom chaosAll resilience patterns
Compliance (5%)PCI DSS checks, audit log completeness, data retentionCustom validatorsAll compliance requirements

Critical test scenarios for payments:

  • Double-charge prevention (idempotency key test)
  • Partial failure (payment authorized but capture fails → auto-void)
  • Currency conversion edge cases (rounding, very small amounts, very large amounts)
  • Concurrent modifications (two refund requests for the same payment)
  • Payment gateway timeout (Toxiproxy: 30s latency on gateway calls)
  • Reconciliation: daily batch verifying our records match the payment processor's
Scenario 4: Build an API gateway for a multi-tenant SaaS

Problem: Your SaaS has 12 backend services and needs a unified API gateway. Design it.

Requirements gathered:

  • Multi-tenant: 500 customers with different rate limits per plan
  • Authentication: OAuth 2.0 / API keys
  • Rate limiting: per-tenant, per-plan tiers
  • Routing: path-based to 12 services
  • Observability: per-tenant metrics, request logging

Decision: Build vs buy:

OptionProsConsVerdict
Custom (Envoy + Lua)Full controlHigh maintenance burdenOnly if unique requirements
KongPlugin ecosystem, Lua extensibilityComplexity at scaleGood for most cases
AWS API GatewayZero ops, native AWS integrationVendor lock-in, cost at scaleGood for AWS-native

Recommended: Kong with custom plugins for multi-tenant rate limiting and per-tenant metrics. Reasons: extensible, proven at scale, team has Lua/Kong experience, and we need custom tenant-aware routing that managed gateways don't support natively.

Interview Strategy

Q: What are the most common mistakes in scenario interviews?
  1. Jumping to solutions — Not asking clarifying questions. Shows junior thinking.
  2. Over-engineering — Proposing Kubernetes + Kafka + service mesh for a problem that needs a cron job and a queue.
  3. Single solution, no alternatives — Not discussing tradeoffs. Staff engineers always consider alternatives.
  4. Ignoring operational concerns — Designing a system but not addressing how it's deployed, monitored, or debugged.
  5. Not drawing diagrams — Even rough boxes and arrows on a whiteboard clarify thinking enormously.
  6. Forgetting failure modes — "What happens when X fails?" is always a follow-up question. Address it proactively.
  7. Trying to be perfect — The interviewer wants to see your thought process, not a production-ready design. It's OK to say "I'd need to benchmark this to choose between A and B."
Q: How do you manage time in a 45-minute scenario interview?
PhaseTimeGoal
Understand + Clarify5 minDefine scope, ask 3-5 key questions
High-level design10 minArchitecture diagram, major components
Deep dive on key component15 minDetail the most complex/interesting part
Tradeoffs + alternatives10 minDiscuss what you considered and rejected
Questions5 minVerify with interviewer, ask follow-ups

Tip: If the interviewer wants to go deep on something, follow their lead. The time allocation is a guide, not a rule. Reading the interviewer's signals is a staff-level skill itself.
