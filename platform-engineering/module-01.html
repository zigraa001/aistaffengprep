Module 1: Platform Engineering Fundamentals

What You'll Learn

  • How platform engineering differs from SRE, DevOps, and infrastructure engineering
  • Internal Developer Platforms (IDPs) and platform-as-a-product thinking
  • Conway's Law, Team Topologies, and organizational design for platforms
  • Golden paths vs golden cages — enabling without constraining
  • Staff-level platform strategy: when to build, buy, or skip entirely

Core Concepts & Definitions

Q: What is platform engineering, and why has it emerged as a distinct discipline?

A: Platform engineering is the discipline of designing, building, and maintaining Internal Developer Platforms (IDPs) — self-service toolchains and abstractions that reduce cognitive load on product engineering teams.

It emerged because the "you build it, you run it" DevOps model, while philosophically sound, created an unsustainable cognitive burden. When every team must independently solve CI/CD, observability, secrets management, infrastructure provisioning, and compliance, you get:

  • Duplicated effort — 20 teams each building their own deployment pipeline
  • Inconsistent quality — some teams have great observability, others have none
  • Security gaps — no centralized enforcement of security baselines
  • Slow onboarding — new engineers spend weeks understanding team-specific tooling

Platform engineering solves this by creating a dedicated team that treats internal developers as customers, providing curated, self-service capabilities that encode organizational best practices.

Q: What is an Internal Developer Platform (IDP) and what does it typically contain?

A: An IDP is a curated, self-service layer that sits between developers and the underlying infrastructure/tooling. It provides opinionated defaults while allowing escape hatches for advanced use cases.

A mature IDP typically includes five capability layers:

graph TB DEV[Developer] --> PORTAL[Developer Portal - Backstage/Port/Cortex] PORTAL --> CICD[CI/CD Pipelines - GitHub Actions, ArgoCD] PORTAL --> INFRA[Infrastructure - Terraform, Crossplane] PORTAL --> OBS[Observability - OTel, Grafana] PORTAL --> SEC[Security - Vault, Policy-as-Code] PORTAL --> DATA[Data - Schema Registry, Feature Flags]
  1. Developer Portal — Service catalog, documentation, scaffolding (Backstage, Port, Cortex)
  2. CI/CD & Delivery — Standardized build, test, deploy pipelines
  3. Infrastructure Provisioning — Self-service environments, databases, queues
  4. Observability — Logging, metrics, tracing configured by default
  5. Security & Compliance — Secrets management, policy enforcement, audit trails

Real-world example: Spotify's Backstage is the most well-known open-source developer portal. It started as an internal tool to manage 2,000+ microservices and was open-sourced in 2020. It provides a service catalog, software templates, TechDocs, and a plugin architecture that lets teams extend it.

Platform-as-a-Product Thinking

Q: What does "platform as a product" mean and why is it the defining principle of platform engineering?

A: "Platform as a product" means applying product management disciplines to internal platforms:

  • User research — Interview developer teams about their pain points, observe workflows, run surveys
  • Product roadmap — Prioritize features based on developer impact, not technical coolness
  • Adoption metrics — Track usage, measure time-to-first-deploy, monitor developer satisfaction (DevEx surveys)
  • Feedback loops — Office hours, Slack channels, NPS scores, feature requests
  • Marketing — Internal evangelism, documentation, workshops, migration guides

Why it matters: The biggest failure mode of platform teams is building what they think developers need (often the most technically interesting thing) rather than what developers actually need (often boring but high-leverage improvements). Without product thinking, platform teams become ivory towers building infrastructure nobody uses.

Netflix example: Netflix's platform team measures success by asking: "Can a new engineer deploy a change to production on their first day?" This single question drives their entire platform roadmap, because achieving it requires excellent documentation, simple tooling, safe defaults, and automated guardrails.

Q: What metrics should a platform team track to measure success?

A: Platform metrics fall into four categories:

CategoryMetricsWhy It Matters
Adoption% teams using the platform, new service onboarding rate, voluntary vs mandated usageIf adoption requires mandates, the product isn't good enough
Developer ProductivityTime-to-first-deploy, lead time for changes, deployment frequencyDORA metrics directly measure delivery capability

Staff-level insight: The most important metric is voluntary adoption rate. If teams choose to use your platform without being forced, you're building something valuable. If you need mandates, you have a product problem, not an adoption problem. Track the ratio of voluntary to mandated adoption over time.

Q: What are "golden paths" and how do they differ from "golden cages"?

A: A golden path is an opinionated, well-supported default way to accomplish a task. It's the "paved road" — the easiest, fastest, most secure way to do something, but not the only way.

A golden cage is when the platform mandates the only way to do something, with no escape hatches.

Golden PathGolden Cage
"Here's a Terraform module for a standard service. Use it, or bring your own.""All infrastructure must go through our portal. No Terraform allowed."
"Our CI template handles 90% of cases. Override any step as needed.""You cannot modify the CI pipeline. File a ticket for changes."
"We recommend PostgreSQL and provide managed instances. Need Redis? Here's the self-service form.""Only PostgreSQL is allowed. Other databases require VP approval."

The 80/20 rule: A good platform should handle 80% of use cases with zero friction via golden paths, and make the remaining 20% possible (even if harder) via escape hatches. The key is that stepping off the golden path should require effort, not permission.

Spotify's approach: Spotify calls these "paved roads." Teams can go off-road, but they take on more operational burden. This creates a natural economic incentive to stay on the paved road without creating bureaucratic gatekeeping.

Organizational Design

Q: How does Conway's Law apply to platform engineering?

A: Conway's Law states: "Any organization that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization's communication structure."

For platform engineering, this has profound implications:

  • If you want a unified developer platform, you need a unified platform team (or at least strong coordination)
  • If your CI/CD, observability, and infra teams are separate silos, your platform will feel like three disconnected products
  • The Inverse Conway Maneuver suggests structuring your organization to produce the system architecture you want

Practical example: A company has separate teams for CI/CD, Kubernetes, and observability. Each team builds excellent tools, but the developer experience is fragmented — deploying a new service requires interacting with three different teams' tools, documentation, and Slack channels. Applying the Inverse Conway Maneuver, you reorganize into a single platform team that owns the end-to-end developer experience, with sub-teams focused on delivery, runtime, and observability but sharing a single product vision.

Q: How do Team Topologies apply to platform engineering?

A: Team Topologies (by Matthew Skelton & Manuel Pais) defines four team types and three interaction modes that map perfectly to platform engineering:

Team types:

  • Stream-aligned teams — Product/feature teams that consume the platform
  • Platform teams — Build and maintain the IDP
  • Enabling teams — Help stream-aligned teams adopt new capabilities (e.g., a DevEx team that helps teams migrate to the new CI)
  • Complicated-subsystem teams — Own deeply specialized components (e.g., a database kernel team)

Interaction modes:

  • X-as-a-Service — The ideal mode for a mature platform. Teams consume it via self-service APIs.
  • Collaboration — Temporary mode during platform development. Platform team works closely with stream-aligned teams to understand needs.
  • Facilitating — Enabling teams help stream-aligned teams adopt new platform capabilities.
graph LR SA1[Stream-Aligned Team 1] -->|X-as-a-Service| PT[Platform Team] SA2[Stream-Aligned Team 2] -->|X-as-a-Service| PT SA3[Stream-Aligned Team 3] -->|Collaboration| PT EN[Enabling Team] -->|Facilitating| SA3 PT --> CS[Complicated Subsystem Team]

Staff-level insight: The maturity of a platform can be measured by the interaction mode. Early platforms require heavy collaboration. Mature platforms operate as X-as-a-Service. If your platform team is constantly in collaboration mode, it's either too early or the self-service abstractions aren't good enough.

Strategic Decisions

Q: When should you NOT build an internal developer platform?

A: Not every organization needs a platform. Building one too early or for the wrong reasons is a common and expensive mistake.

Don't build a platform when:

  • You have fewer than ~50 engineers — The overhead of a platform team (3-5 engineers minimum) isn't justified. Use managed services and scripts instead.
  • Teams aren't actually duplicating effort — If each team has unique tooling needs, standardization may create more friction than it solves.
  • You're building for "someday" — Premature platforming is as bad as premature optimization. Build when pain is real and measurable.
  • Leadership wants cost savings, not developer experience — Platforms that are justified purely as cost-cutting measures tend to under-invest in UX and fail to get adoption.
  • Your organization can't fund it properly — A platform team that's a part-time side project for 2 people will produce a half-baked product that erodes trust.

Staff-level framing: In an interview, when asked about platforms, demonstrate that you know when NOT to build one. This shows strategic thinking beyond the technical. Say: "Before advocating for a platform, I'd validate that at least 3 teams are independently solving the same problem, that the total cost of the duplicated effort exceeds the cost of a platform team, and that leadership is committed to funding it as a product, not a project."

Q: How do you approach the build vs buy decision for platform capabilities?

A: The build vs buy decision for platforms should be evaluated on four axes:

FactorFavors BuildFavors Buy
DifferentiationCore to competitive advantageCommodity capability
Integration depthNeeds deep integration with internal systemsWorks well as standalone
Scale/customizationUnique scale or workflow requirementsStandard requirements
Maintenance burdenTeam has capacity to maintain long-termVendor handles upgrades, security patches

Real-world examples:

  • Build: Google built Borg (and later Kubernetes) because container orchestration was core to their operational model at a scale no vendor could match
  • Buy: Most companies should use GitHub Actions or CircleCI for CI/CD rather than building a custom CI system. CI is a commodity.
  • Compose: Use Backstage (open source) as the portal layer, but build custom plugins for your specific workflows. This is the most common and often best approach.

The "thin platform" principle: Prefer composing existing tools (managed services, open-source) with a thin integration layer over building from scratch. Your platform's value is in the curation and integration, not in reimplementing commodity infrastructure.

Staff-Level Strategy

Q: How should a staff engineer approach proposing a platform initiative?

A: A staff engineer's platform proposal should be structured as a business case, not a technical pitch:

  1. Quantify the problem — "Our 15 product teams each spend ~20% of their time on infrastructure concerns. That's 15 × 0.2 × $200K = $600K/year in duplicated effort."
  2. Show the duplication — Document how many teams have independently built CI pipelines, monitoring dashboards, deployment scripts.
  3. Propose incrementally — Don't propose a grand unified platform. Start with the highest-pain, highest-value capability. "Let's start with standardized CI/CD, which 12 of 15 teams flagged as their top pain point."
  4. Define success metrics — "In 6 months, 80% of teams should be on the standard pipeline, and mean deployment time should drop from 45 minutes to 10."
  5. Address risks — Platform team becomes a bottleneck, over-engineering, forcing adoption too early.

Common mistake: Leading with technical architecture instead of business impact. Executives don't care about your Backstage plugin architecture — they care that developers ship faster and more safely.

Q: What's the "thinnest viable platform" concept and why does it matter?

A: The "thinnest viable platform" (TVP) is the minimum platform needed to accelerate delivery for stream-aligned teams. It's the MVP concept applied to platform engineering.

Why it matters: Platform teams often fall into the trap of building a comprehensive platform before shipping anything. They spend 12 months building the "perfect" developer portal, CI/CD system, and infrastructure layer — and by the time they ship, the organization has moved on or lost faith.

TVP in practice:

  • Week 1-2: A shared Slack channel and a wiki page documenting existing best practices
  • Month 1: A standardized CI/CD template that teams can adopt voluntarily
  • Month 3: A basic service catalog (even a spreadsheet) and a self-service environment provisioning script
  • Month 6: Backstage or Port with service catalog, CI integration, and basic scaffolding
  • Month 12: Full IDP with self-service infrastructure, observability, and security baseline

The TVP could literally be a set of well-curated documentation and Terraform modules. Not every platform needs a portal on day one.

Q: How do you handle platform adoption resistance from engineering teams?

A: Adoption resistance usually comes from one of four sources, each requiring a different strategy:

Source of ResistanceRoot CauseStrategy
"Not invented here"Teams proud of their custom toolingAcknowledge their work. Offer to incorporate their best ideas into the platform. Make them contributors, not consumers.
"It doesn't fit our needs"Legitimate unique requirementsValidate the requirement. If real, add escape hatches. If perceived, demonstrate how the platform handles their case.
"Last platform failed"Historical trauma from bad platformsStart small, deliver value quickly, build trust incrementally. Transparent roadmap and feedback loops.
"We don't have time to migrate"Migration cost is realProvide migration tooling and support. Offer "white glove" migration for early adopters. Never mandate without migration resources.

The key principle: You should never need to mandate platform adoption if the platform is genuinely better than the alternative. If teams aren't adopting voluntarily, that's a product signal, not a compliance problem. The exception is security/compliance requirements, which may justify mandates.

Q: What are the biggest failure modes of platform engineering teams?

A:

  1. Building without user research — The "Field of Dreams" fallacy: "If we build it, they will come." They won't. Interview your developer customers first.
  2. Over-abstracting too early — Creating generic frameworks when you have 2 use cases. Wait for at least 3 concrete examples before abstracting.
  3. Mandating without value — Forcing teams onto a platform that's worse than their current setup destroys trust permanently.
  4. Treating it as a project, not a product — Building a platform and then disbanding the team. Platforms need ongoing investment, like any product.
  5. Ignoring documentation — A platform without excellent docs is a platform without users. Invest 20% of effort in documentation.
  6. Centralizing everything — Not every capability needs to be on the platform. Some things are better left to individual teams.
  7. Neglecting the migration path — Building the new system without a clear, supported migration path from the old one.

Staff-level interview tip: Demonstrating awareness of these failure modes shows maturity. When discussing platform design in an interview, proactively mention how you'd mitigate 2-3 of these risks.

Q: How do you measure the ROI of a platform engineering investment?

A: Platform ROI is notoriously hard to measure because the benefits are diffuse. Here's a practical framework:

Direct savings (easier to measure):

  • Reduction in infrastructure costs through standardization
  • Reduction in support tickets (fewer "how do I deploy?" questions)
  • Faster incident resolution through consistent observability

Productivity gains (harder but more impactful):

  • Developer time reclaimed from infrastructure tasks (survey before/after)
  • Faster onboarding (measure time-to-first-PR, time-to-first-deploy)
  • Increased deployment frequency (DORA metrics)

Risk reduction (hardest to quantify):

  • Fewer security incidents from consistent security baselines
  • Faster compliance audits through standardized controls
  • Reduced blast radius from consistent deployment practices

A practical formula:

Platform ROI = (Hours saved per team per week × Number of teams × Hourly rate × 52)
               + (Incident cost reduction)
               + (Compliance audit time savings)
               - (Platform team cost)
               - (Migration costs)

Example: 15 teams × 8 hours/week saved × $100/hour × 52 weeks = $624K/year in productivity gains alone. If the platform team costs $800K/year (4 engineers), you need to show additional value from incident reduction and compliance to justify the investment.
