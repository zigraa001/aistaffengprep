Module 7: The Big Three Questions

Learning Objectives

  • Craft a compelling 60-90 second "Tell Me About Yourself" that steers the interview toward your strengths
  • Select and present your most impactful project using the CARL framework at staff-level depth
  • Structure a conflict resolution story that demonstrates maturity, empathy, and growth
  • Prepare thoughtful questions to ask the interviewer that demonstrate research and genuine curiosity

Why These Three?

Across Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, Netflix, and Stripe, these three questions appear in virtually every behavioral interview loop. They are so common that not having polished answers is a red flag. Yet most candidates underprepare them, treating them as "easy warm-ups" rather than the high-signal opportunities they are. At staff level, these questions carry even more weight because they reveal how you think about your career trajectory, what you value in your work, and how you handle the interpersonal complexity that defines senior technical leadership.

1. Tell Me About Yourself (TMAY)

Why TMAY Is Your Most Important 90 Seconds ▼

TMAY is not a throwaway icebreaker. It is the one moment in the interview where you have complete control over the narrative. Every other question is reactive; this one is proactive. A strong TMAY accomplishes three things simultaneously:

  • Breaks tension: Both you and the interviewer are settling in. A confident, well-structured opening puts everyone at ease.
  • Establishes context: You frame your career arc so subsequent stories make sense. Instead of explaining context each time, you can say "remember when I mentioned the platform migration..."
  • Steers the conversation: The topics you mention become the topics the interviewer asks about next. If you highlight your distributed systems expertise, expect follow-ups there. This is your chance to guide them toward your strongest signal areas.

Part 1: Personal Summary (10-15 seconds)

State your current role, your area of expertise, and your years of relevant experience. Be specific rather than generic. Do not recite your resume chronologically.

Good Example

"I'm a staff engineer at Contoso, where I lead the platform infrastructure team. For the past 8 years I've focused on building distributed systems that serve hundreds of millions of users."

Weak Example

"So I graduated from State University in 2014 with a CS degree, and then I got my first job at a small startup..."

This starts a chronological walk-through that will take too long and buries the lead.

Part 2: Key Accomplishments (40-60 seconds)

Pick 2-3 career highlights that demonstrate staff-level impact. Each should be one or two sentences. Choose accomplishments that map to what the target role requires. These become "breadcrumbs" the interviewer will follow up on.

Staff-Level Tip: Strategic Breadcrumbs

Each accomplishment you mention is an invitation for the interviewer to ask "Tell me more about that." Only mention projects where you have deep, impressive stories ready to tell. If your strongest story is about conflict resolution during a migration, mention the migration. The interviewer will almost certainly follow the thread you lay down.

Part 3: Forward-Looking Statement (10-15 seconds)

Connect your background to why you are excited about this specific role. This should feel genuine, not flattering. Reference something concrete about the company or team.

Good Example

"I'm particularly excited about [Company] because you're tackling the same kind of scale challenges I love solving, and the opportunity to shape the platform direction at this stage of growth is exactly where I want to be."

Weak Example

"I've always admired your company and think it would be a great place to work."

Generic flattery that could apply to any company. Shows no research.

Complete TMAY Examples for Different Backgrounds ▼

Example 1: Big Tech Background

"I'm a senior software engineer at Microsoft on the Azure Kubernetes team, where I've spent the last four years building control plane infrastructure that manages over 10 million containers globally.

Two highlights that define my work: First, I led the redesign of our cluster autoscaling system, which reduced provisioning latency by 60% and saved $40M annually in compute costs across the fleet. That required aligning six teams across two orgs and making hard tradeoffs between latency, cost, and reliability. Second, I drove the adoption of a new observability platform across our org of 200 engineers. I built the initial proof of concept, wrote the migration playbook, and mentored three teams through the transition.

I'm excited about this staff role at [Company] because you're building foundational platform services at a scale where the architecture decisions genuinely matter, and that's exactly the kind of ambiguity I thrive in."

Example 2: Startup Background

"I'm a founding engineer at a Series B fintech startup, where I've worn many hats over the past five years, from designing our core payment processing pipeline to building and leading a team of eight engineers.

The work I'm most proud of: I architected our real-time fraud detection system from scratch, which now processes 50,000 transactions per second and has prevented over $12M in fraudulent charges. This required deeply understanding the business domain, building ML-informed rule engines, and designing for five nines of availability because downtime literally costs money. I also led the effort to break our monolith into microservices, which was as much an organizational challenge as a technical one.

I'm drawn to [Company] because you're at the stage where early architectural decisions will compound for years, and my experience scaling systems from zero to millions of users maps directly to the challenges you're facing."

Example 3: Career Changer (Non-Traditional Path)

"I'm a staff engineer at a healthcare tech company. I actually started my career as a mechanical engineer, which gave me a strong foundation in systems thinking and optimization before I transitioned into software seven years ago.

That cross-domain perspective has been a real differentiator. I led the design of our patient data pipeline that processes 2TB of medical records daily while maintaining HIPAA compliance end-to-end. That project required bringing together security, infrastructure, and product teams who had very different priorities. More recently, I identified that our deployment process was our biggest bottleneck and championed a platform engineering initiative that cut deployment time from 4 hours to 15 minutes across the organization.

What excites me about [Company] is your focus on developer productivity at scale. I've seen firsthand how the right platform investments can unlock engineering velocity, and I'd love to bring that experience to your team."

"I'm a Staff Software Engineer at Microsoft working on the Word Copilot team, where I lead the AI infrastructure that powers intelligent document experiences for millions of Microsoft 365 users.

My Story: Most Impactful Project — Word Copilot RAG Pipeline (Microsoft — 2023-2024) ▼

Context (CARL format): "Word Copilot was generating responses grounded in user documents, but our retrieval pipeline had a 12% hallucination rate — meaning roughly 1 in 8 AI-assisted edits contained information not actually in the user's document. For an enterprise product used by millions, this was a trust-eroding problem."

Action:

  • "I identified the root cause: our chunking strategy was losing document structure context. Tables, headers, and cross-references were being split in ways that destroyed semantic meaning."
  • "I proposed a structure-aware chunking approach to our research partners and co-designed the algorithm with them — this required building trust across the product/research boundary, since research teams typically drove algorithmic decisions."
  • "Coordinated with the platform team to redesign the indexing pipeline — this was the hardest alignment challenge because it required them to reprioritize their Q3 roadmap."
  • "Led phased rollout: internal dogfood → 1% canary → 10% → GA over 14 months. Each phase had clear go/no-go metrics I defined upfront."
  • "Built an evaluation framework with automated hallucination detection that became the standard for measuring RAG quality across all Copilot products."

Result: "Hallucination rate dropped from 12% to 3%. The structure-aware chunking approach was adopted by Excel and PowerPoint Copilot teams. The evaluation framework I built saved an estimated 6 engineer-months of duplicated work across the org. My manager cited this project as the primary evidence in my staff-level promotion case."

Learning: "The technical solution was maybe 30% of the work. The other 70% was alignment — getting research to co-own the approach, getting platform to reprioritize, and getting PM to accept a 14-month phased timeline instead of a big-bang launch. I learned that at staff level, the architecture of the collaboration matters as much as the architecture of the system."

Why This Works at Staff Level

  • Cross-org scope: Product, Research, and Platform teams — not just your own team
  • Quantified impact: 12% → 3% hallucination, 6 engineer-months saved, promotion evidence
  • Shows judgment: Phased rollout with defined go/no-go metrics, not just "shipped it"
  • Multiplier effect: Evaluation framework and chunking approach adopted org-wide
  • Self-aware reflection: "30% technical, 70% alignment" shows staff-level thinking
My Story: Conflict Resolution — Copilot Reliability vs. Features (Microsoft — 2024) ▼

Setup: "Two weeks before a major Copilot rollout to enterprise customers, I identified that our P99 latency had crept above the 3.5-second SLA threshold. The PM wanted to push forward with a new summarization feature that would add ~200ms of latency. I believed we needed to freeze features and fix reliability first."

Internal Process: "My first instinct was to escalate — this felt urgent. But I recognized that would damage my relationship with the PM and bypass the collaboration that staff engineers are expected to model. I also checked my own bias: was I being too conservative? I validated with our SRE data that the latency trend was accelerating, not stabilizing."

Approach:

  • "Scheduled a 1:1 with the PM instead of raising it in the team meeting — gave them space to think without feeling publicly challenged."
  • "Came with data, not opinions: latency trend graphs, SLA breach projections, and customer escalation patterns."
  • "Proposed a hybrid sprint: 60% reliability work, 40% feature work, instead of an all-or-nothing choice."
  • "Offered to build a reliability dashboard the PM could show to their leadership — made it easy for them to advocate for the pause."
  • "Root-caused the latency to a cache eviction issue and proposed adaptive TTLs as the fix — showed this wasn't an open-ended investigation."

Outcome: "We ran the hybrid sprint. Error rates dropped 35% in one week. P99 latency returned to under 3.5s. The summarization feature shipped two weeks later — and launched on a stable foundation. The PM later told me they appreciated that I brought solutions, not just problems."

Learning: "The instinct to escalate in urgent situations is strong, but at staff level, your job is to create alignment, not force decisions. By bringing data and a compromise, I turned a potential conflict into a collaboration. I also learned that making it easy for the other person to say yes — the dashboard for their leadership — is often more effective than being right."

Why This Works at Staff Level

  • 5-part conflict structure: Setup → Internal process → Approach → Outcome → Learning
  • Shows self-awareness: "Checked my own bias" and considered escalation trade-offs
  • Win-win resolution: Hybrid sprint, not win-lose
  • Empathy-driven influence: Built dashboard to help PM advocate, private 1:1 instead of public challenge
  • Technical credibility: Root-caused the issue (cache eviction) — not just process talk

2. Tell Me About Your Favorite / Most Impactful Project

Why This Question Is a Staff-Level Litmus Test ▼

This question reveals more about a candidate than almost any other. What you choose to highlight says as much as how you describe it. Interviewers are evaluating several things simultaneously:

  • What you value: Do you pick a project because of technical elegance, business impact, team growth, or organizational change? Staff engineers should value impact over cleverness.
  • Scope of thinking: A senior engineer describes a feature. A staff engineer describes a system that enabled multiple teams to ship faster.
  • Self-awareness: Can you articulate what you learned, what you would do differently, and how you grew?
  • Passion: Genuine enthusiasm is infectious and hard to fake. Pick a project you actually care about.

How to Choose the Right Project

Your project should demonstrate at least three of these staff-level signals:

Cross-Team Impact

The project affected multiple teams, required coordination, and solved a problem beyond your team's boundary.

Ambiguity Navigation

The problem space was unclear. You shaped the solution, not just implemented a spec handed to you.

Measurable Business Impact

Revenue, cost savings, user growth, reliability improvements with actual numbers.

Lasting Systemic Change

The project created patterns, platforms, or processes that continued to deliver value long after you finished.

Mentoring & Growth

You grew other engineers through the project. Junior engineers leveled up. Knowledge transferred.

Technical Depth

You can go 3-4 levels deep on any aspect if asked. You understand the tradeoffs intimately.

Structuring Your Response with CARL ▼

The CARL framework (Context, Actions, Results, Learnings) works particularly well for this question because the "Learnings" component naturally demonstrates the self-awareness interviewers are looking for.

CARL Breakdown for Project Stories

Component Time What to Include Staff-Level Signal
Context 30 sec Business problem, scale, why it mattered, who was involved You understand the business, not just the code
Actions 90 sec Your specific contributions, decisions made, tradeoffs, how you brought people along Technical depth + leadership + decision-making under uncertainty
Results 30 sec Quantified impact, business outcomes, team outcomes You measure success in business terms, not just technical terms
Learnings 30 sec What you would do differently, what surprised you, how it changed your approach Self-awareness, growth mindset, intellectual humility

Full Worked Example: Strong Response

Context: "My most impactful project was redesigning the notification delivery system at [Company]. We served 200 million users, but our notification pipeline had become a bottleneck. Delivery latency had crept up to 45 seconds, engagement rates were dropping, and the system was a patchwork of five years of incremental additions. Three product teams were blocked from launching features because the pipeline couldn't handle their requirements."

Actions:

  • "I started by mapping the entire delivery chain end-to-end, which nobody had done in years. I found that 70% of the latency came from a single synchronous database call that was checking user notification preferences on every send."
  • "I proposed a redesign using an event-driven architecture with a pre-computed preference cache. The key tradeoff was accepting slightly stale preference data (up to 60 seconds) in exchange for eliminating the synchronous lookup. I wrote an RFC documenting the alternatives, including a CDC-based approach that would have been more consistent but required 3x the engineering effort."
  • "The hardest part wasn't technical. The mobile team was deeply skeptical because a previous migration had caused a week of push notification failures. I organized a series of joint design sessions, incorporated their feedback into the rollout plan, and agreed to a gradual migration starting with their lowest-risk notification channel."
  • "I led the implementation across three teams over two quarters. I personally built the cache invalidation system and the migration tooling, while mentoring two mid-level engineers on the event processing pipeline."

Results: "We reduced P99 delivery latency from 45 seconds to 800 milliseconds. Engagement rates on push notifications increased 12%, which the product team estimated was worth $8M in annual revenue. The three blocked product teams launched their features within a month of the migration completing. The new architecture has been running for two years now with zero major incidents."

Learnings: "The biggest lesson was that the technical design was maybe 30% of the challenge. Getting buy-in from the mobile team was 70%. If I did it again, I'd bring them in even earlier, during the problem analysis phase rather than after I had a proposed solution. I also learned that writing a thorough RFC saved us weeks of back-and-forth in meetings. That experience is why I now write an RFC for any project that touches more than two teams."

Why This Response Works
  • Starts with business context (engagement dropping), not technical details
  • Shows end-to-end ownership: analysis, design, alignment, implementation, mentoring
  • Explicitly discusses tradeoffs (stale data vs. latency)
  • Addresses the human/organizational challenge alongside the technical one
  • Quantifies results in both technical and business terms
  • Learnings show genuine self-awareness, not false modesty
Common Mistakes with Project Stories ▼

Mistake 1: Too Technical, No Business Context

"I migrated our system from Kafka to Pulsar because Pulsar supports multi-tenancy natively and has tiered storage..."

This sounds like a tech talk, not an impact story. The interviewer is wondering: why did it matter? What business problem did it solve? Who cared?

Mistake 2: No Personal Contribution

"Our team built a new recommendation engine that increased click-through rates by 15%."

"Our team" hides your individual role. Interviewers need to know what YOU specifically did. Use "I" and describe your unique contributions.

Mistake 3: No Reflection or Learnings

Ending with "and it was a big success" and nothing else.

Missing the self-awareness component. Staff engineers must show they learn from every experience, including successes.

Mistake 4: Choosing a Solo Technical Achievement

"I wrote a really elegant distributed lock implementation using Redis."

While technically impressive, this shows senior engineer scope, not staff engineer scope. Staff-level projects involve organizational complexity, not just technical complexity.

3. Tell Me About a Time You Resolved a Conflict

Why Tech Companies Test Conflict Resolution ▼

Conflict is inevitable in software engineering. Staff engineers encounter it constantly: disagreements on architecture, priority conflicts between teams, resource allocation battles, technical debt vs. feature pressure, and interpersonal friction. Companies test conflict resolution because:

  • Conflict avoidance is expensive: Unresolved disagreements lead to duplicated systems, passive-aggressive behavior, and teams that don't collaborate. The cost compounds.
  • Staff engineers are expected to resolve, not escalate: At senior levels, running to your manager every time there's a disagreement is a red flag. You should be the one who resolves conflicts, or at least exhausts all reasonable options before escalating.
  • Technical disagreements are proxy for values: How you handle conflict reveals your communication style, your ego management, your ability to separate ideas from identity, and your capacity for empathy.

Choosing the Right Conflict Story

Not all conflicts make good interview stories. Here is how to select one:

Use This Kind of Conflict Avoid This Kind of Conflict
Professional disagreement with real stakes Petty interpersonal drama
Conflict where you grew or changed your mind Conflict where you were clearly "right" and the other person was "wrong"
Cross-team technical disagreement Conflict with your manager about a promotion
Resolved with a win-win outcome Resolved by someone else or by someone leaving
Preserved or strengthened the relationship Ended the working relationship
The Conflict Resolution Story Structure ▼

Unlike a typical STAR story, conflict stories benefit from a five-part structure that emphasizes your internal thought process and the relationship outcome.

What Interviewers Want to See

  • Empathy: You tried to understand the other person's perspective and motivations, not just their stated position
  • Maturity: You managed your own emotions. You did not respond in anger or frustration
  • Seeking to understand first: You asked questions before advocating your position
  • Win-win orientation: You looked for solutions that addressed both parties' underlying needs
  • Relationship preservation: The working relationship was maintained or improved after the conflict
  • Ownership: You took responsibility for your part in the conflict, even if the other person was mostly at fault

Full Worked Example: Strong Response

Setup: "I had a significant technical disagreement with the tech lead of the data platform team about how to handle a critical data migration. We were moving our user analytics pipeline from batch to stream processing. I wanted to use a strangler fig pattern with a dual-write phase, which would take 8 weeks but allow zero-downtime migration. She wanted a big-bang cutover during a maintenance window, which would take 3 weeks but required 4 hours of downtime. Both approaches had merit, but we were stuck and the project timeline was at risk."

Internal Process: "My initial reaction was frustration, honestly. I felt like the risk of a big-bang migration was obvious. But I caught myself and realized I was anchoring on my past experience with a failed cutover at a previous company, and that was coloring my judgment. I also recognized that she had far more context on the data platform's capabilities than I did."

Resolution Approach:

  • "I asked her to coffee, one-on-one, away from our teams. I explicitly said I wanted to understand her perspective before pushing mine."
  • "I learned that her team had done six successful big-bang migrations in the past year with their mature runbook process. She wasn't being reckless; she had data showing it worked. My concern about downtime was valid but her risk was lower than I assumed."
  • "I shared my specific concern: we had 15 downstream consumers, and if anything went wrong during cutover, we'd have cascading failures. She hadn't considered the downstream consumer count."
  • "Together, we designed a hybrid approach: big-bang migration for the core pipeline (her preference) but with a pre-built rollback mechanism and a staged consumer migration over two weeks (addressing my concern). The total timeline was 5 weeks."

Outcome: "The migration completed successfully in 5 weeks with zero incidents. The hybrid approach was actually better than either of our original proposals. More importantly, that conversation built a strong working relationship. She and I co-authored a migration playbook that became the standard for the organization. We've collaborated on three more projects since then."

Learning: "I learned that technical disagreements are almost never about the tech. They're about different assumptions and different past experiences. Now, when I find myself in a disagreement, my first move is always to ask 'What do you know that I don't?' instead of trying to convince. I also learned the value of one-on-one conversations over group meetings for resolving conflicts. People are more flexible when they don't feel like they're losing face in front of their team."

Why This Response Works
  • Acknowledges the other person's perspective was valid, not just tolerated
  • Shows emotional self-awareness ("My initial reaction was frustration")
  • Describes a specific resolution process, not just "we talked it out"
  • The outcome was genuinely better than either original proposal
  • Relationship was strengthened, not just preserved
  • Learning is actionable and specific, not a generic platitude
Conflict Resolution Anti-Patterns ▼

These patterns will damage your evaluation, even if the underlying story is decent:

Anti-Pattern: The Blame Story

"My colleague kept making bad architectural decisions and I had to step in to fix them."

This makes you sound difficult to work with. Even if the other person was wrong, a staff engineer shows empathy and seeks to understand why they made those decisions.

Anti-Pattern: The Conflict Avoider

"I just let it go because it wasn't worth fighting about."

This is not conflict resolution; it's conflict avoidance. Staff engineers address disagreements constructively rather than letting them fester or giving in to avoid discomfort.

Anti-Pattern: Escalation as First Resort

"We couldn't agree, so I brought it to our VP and she decided."

Escalation is a valid tool, but only after you've genuinely tried to resolve it yourself. Describing immediate escalation signals that you can't handle peer-level disagreements.

Anti-Pattern: No Follow-Up

"We agreed on an approach and moved on."

What happened to the relationship? Did you check in? Did the solution work? Staff engineers close the loop. Mentioning ongoing collaboration or a stronger relationship signals maturity.

Anti-Pattern: The "I Was Right" Story

"I eventually convinced them that my approach was correct."

Even if true, this framing makes you sound like you steamroll people. The best conflict stories end with a solution that incorporated both perspectives.

Bonus: Questions to Ask the Interviewer

Why Your Questions Matter More Than You Think ▼

The last five minutes of every interview round are typically reserved for your questions. Many candidates treat this as an afterthought or skip it entirely. This is a missed opportunity. Your questions reveal your priorities, your depth of thinking, and whether you've done your homework. At staff level, asking no questions or asking superficial ones is a negative signal. It suggests either a lack of curiosity or a lack of preparation, both of which are disqualifying traits for senior technical leadership.

10 Strong Questions (Organized by Category)

Role & Expectations
  1. "What does the first 90 days look like for someone in this role? What would success look like at the 6-month mark?" — Shows you think about ramp-up and measurable impact, not just getting the offer.
  2. "What's the biggest technical challenge the team is facing right now that this role would help address?" — Signals you're already thinking about how to contribute, not just evaluating the opportunity.
  3. "How is the balance between hands-on coding and broader technical leadership for staff engineers here?" — Demonstrates awareness that staff roles vary dramatically by company.
Team & Collaboration
  1. "Can you describe a recent cross-team project and how the collaboration worked?" — Reveals organizational health and how well teams actually work together.
  2. "How does the team handle technical disagreements? Is there a formal design review process?" — Shows you care about decision-making quality, not just who makes decisions.
Culture & Growth
  1. "What's something the team has learned from a recent failure or incident?" — Tests psychological safety. If they can't name one, that's a signal.
  2. "How does the organization think about technical debt? Is there dedicated time for it, or is it woven into feature work?" — Reveals engineering maturity and whether they value long-term health.
  3. "What does the path from staff to principal look like here? Are there staff engineers who've made that transition recently?" — Shows ambition and long-term thinking about your career at the company.
Technical & Strategic
  1. "What's the team's biggest bet for the next year, and what are you most uncertain about in that bet?" — Encourages the interviewer to share real challenges, not just the polished pitch.
  2. "If you could magically fix one thing about the codebase or architecture tomorrow, what would it be?" — Fun question that reveals real pain points and gives you genuine insight into the work.

5 Questions to Avoid

Question Why It's Problematic
"What does your company do?" Shows you did zero research. Instantly disqualifying at staff level.
"What's the salary range?" Compensation questions go to the recruiter, not the interviewer. Asking during a technical round seems transactional.
"I don't have any questions." Signals disinterest or lack of intellectual curiosity. Always have at least two prepared.
"How many hours a week do people work?" Legitimate concern, but phrasing sounds like you're looking for minimal effort. Ask about work-life balance more tactfully.
"Did I get the job?" Puts the interviewer in an awkward position. The process will run its course.

Tailoring Questions to the Interviewer

Interviewer Role Best Topics Avoid
Recruiter Process, timeline, team structure, compensation, benefits, what the interview loop looks like Deep technical questions they can't answer
Peer Engineer Day-to-day work, tech stack, code review process, team dynamics, interesting technical challenges Organizational strategy they don't influence
Engineering Manager Team goals, growth opportunities, how staff engineers partner with management, performance evaluation Complaints about process at your current company
Director / VP Org strategy, biggest bets, how engineering and product collaborate, company vision Granular technical implementation details
Pro Tip: Turn Questions Into Conversations

The best "reverse interview" questions lead to a genuine conversation, not a one-line answer. When the interviewer responds, ask a thoughtful follow-up. For example, after asking about the biggest technical challenge, you might say "That's interesting, at my current company we faced something similar. We approached it by... How does your team think about that tradeoff?" This transforms the Q&A into a peer discussion, which is exactly the signal you want to send at staff level.

Putting It All Together: Your Preparation Checklist

Practice Plan for the Big Three ▼

Week 1: Draft and Refine

  • Write out your TMAY word-for-word. Time it. Cut until it is 60-90 seconds.
  • Choose your top project and draft the CARL response. Aim for 3 minutes when spoken.
  • Select your conflict story. Write out all five parts of the structure.
  • Prepare 6-8 questions for interviewers, organized by interviewer role.

Week 2: Pressure Test

  • Record yourself delivering each response. Listen back for filler words, pacing, and clarity.
  • Practice with a friend or partner. Ask them: "What did you learn about me? What's the one thing you'd ask me a follow-up about?"
  • For each response, prepare for two likely follow-up questions and rehearse answers.
  • Practice transitioning smoothly between responses. The interviewer might follow your TMAY breadcrumb directly into your project story.

Week 3: Adapt and Customize

  • Research each company you're interviewing with. Customize your forward-looking statement in TMAY.
  • Adjust your project story emphasis based on the role (more technical depth for a systems role, more leadership for a tech lead role).
  • Prepare company-specific questions for the reverse interview segment.
The Recording Test

Record yourself delivering your TMAY and play it back. If you cringe, that's normal, but if you notice you're saying "um" more than twice, or your story takes longer than 2 minutes, or you sound like you're reading a script, you need more practice. The goal is "rehearsed but natural," like a keynote speaker who knows their material but sounds conversational.
