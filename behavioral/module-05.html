Module 5: Failure & Growth Mindset

Learning Objectives

  • Select appropriate failure stories
  • Own failures without excuses
  • Demonstrate concrete learning and change
  • Show self-awareness and humility

Choosing Good Failures

Good Failures to Discuss ▼
  • Technical decisions with unexpected consequences
  • Communication failures
  • Delayed escalation of issues
  • Underestimating complexity
  • Missing important stakeholder concerns

Criteria:

  • Significant enough to show real learning
  • Clearly resolved (not ongoing)
  • Shows growth trajectory
  • Doesn't reveal serious judgment lapses
Failures to Avoid Discussing ▼
  • Ethical violations
  • Ongoing interpersonal conflicts
  • Things showing poor judgment on safety/security
  • Situations where you learned nothing
  • Very recent unresolved issues

Common Failure Questions

"Tell me about a project that failed" ▼

What the interviewer is testing: Can you take ownership of a project-level failure and show systemic learning, not just personal regret?

Structure your answer around:

  • Project context and goal: Brief setup -- what you were building and why it mattered
  • What went wrong: Be specific. Was it scope creep, wrong technical choice, misaligned stakeholders, or poor estimation?
  • Your role in the failure: At staff level, this means owning the leadership and decision-making failures, not just coding errors. Did you fail to escalate early? Did you miss signals from the team?
  • What you learned and changed: Show process-level improvements, not just "I'll try harder." Did you introduce pre-mortems, change how you do discovery, or create new review processes?

Staff-level tip: The best answers show you changed how your team or organization operates, not just your personal behavior. For example: "I introduced mandatory technical discovery sprints before any commitment" is stronger than "I learned to be more careful."

"Describe your biggest professional mistake" ▼

What the interviewer is testing: Self-awareness, humility, and the ability to be vulnerable without being self-destructive.

Structure your answer around:

  • What was the mistake? Choose something with real consequences -- not a trivial error, but not a fireable offense either
  • Impact on team/project/company: Quantify if possible. "Delayed launch by 3 weeks" or "caused 2 hours of downtime" shows you understand the blast radius
  • How you addressed it: Immediate remediation actions. Did you own it publicly? Did you help fix it?
  • Long-term changes made: Systemic fixes that prevent recurrence

Staff-level tip: Good mistakes for staff interviews involve judgment calls -- choosing the wrong architecture, underinvesting in a critical area, or failing to communicate a risk upward. These show you operate at a strategic level.

"What's the toughest feedback you've received?" ▼

What the interviewer is testing: Coachability and emotional maturity. Can you hear hard truths and grow from them?

Structure your answer around:

  • What was the feedback? Be specific and honest. Vague answers ("someone said I could communicate better") sound evasive
  • Initial reaction (be honest): It's OK to say "My first reaction was defensiveness" -- this shows self-awareness. Don't claim you immediately embraced it
  • How you processed it: Did you reflect, seek more data from others, or discuss it with a mentor? Show a mature processing approach
  • Actions taken and results: Concrete behavioral changes with observable outcomes

Staff-level tip: The most impressive answers involve feedback about leadership behaviors -- "You're not creating enough space for others to lead" or "You're too deep in the code and not focused enough on strategy" -- because these show you're operating at the right altitude and are willing to evolve your identity as an engineer.

Example Response

Good Failure Response

Own it: "I made a significant mistake when I underestimated a migration project."

Context: "I was leading a database migration and committed to a two-week timeline."

What happened: "We ended up taking six weeks. This delayed a product launch and frustrated stakeholders."

Reflection: "I failed in two ways: I didn't consult enough with affected teams, and I gave an estimate before proper discovery."

Changes made: "Since then, I always do a week of discovery before committing to timelines, and I explicitly identify dependencies and get sign-off from affected teams."

Application: "Last quarter, I applied this to a similar migration that we completed on time."

Sher's Personalized Stories

My Story: Agent Sandboxing Underestimation (Microsoft — 2024) ▼

Best for: "Tell me about a time you failed" or "Describe a project that didn't go as planned"

Own it: "I significantly underestimated the complexity of building the sandboxing layer for our Agent Harness Platform."

Context: "When I proposed the shared agent harness for Copilot teams, I estimated the sandboxing component — secure container execution for AI agent actions — would take 4 weeks. It ended up taking 10 weeks, nearly derailing the project timeline and delaying the Excel team's onboarding."

What happened:

  • "I underestimated the security review process — AppSec required three full review cycles because LLM-driven code execution was a novel threat model with no existing playbook"
  • "Container cold-start latency was 8 seconds — unacceptable for interactive Copilot features. Optimizing it to under 2 seconds required fundamentally rethinking the warm pool strategy"
  • "The isolation requirements were more complex than anticipated — agents needed to read user documents but not write to other users' files, requiring fine-grained permission boundaries I hadn't scoped"

Reflection: "I failed in two ways. First, I estimated based on the 'happy path' — I knew sandboxing conceptually but didn't account for the security review overhead or the performance optimization work. Second, I gave a confident single-point estimate ('4 weeks') instead of a range with assumptions, which set unrealistic expectations with stakeholders."

Changes made:

  • "I now run mandatory 'discovery sprints' for any component touching security or novel infrastructure — 1 week of prototyping before committing to estimates"
  • "I switched to confidence-level estimates: 'I'm 70% confident it's 6 weeks, 90% confident it's 10 weeks' — this sets better expectations and surfaces risk early"
  • "I include security review lead time explicitly in all project plans — it's never 'a quick sign-off'"
  • "For the next platform component, I applied this approach and estimated within 10% of actual delivery time"

Application: "When I scoped the observability layer for the same platform, I ran a 1-week discovery sprint, identified that the distributed tracing integration was the high-risk component, and gave a range estimate. We delivered on time."

Anticipated Follow-Up Questions

Q: "How did stakeholders react to the 6-week delay?"

A: "I communicated the revised timeline within 2 weeks of recognizing the miss — I didn't wait until week 10. I framed it as: 'The security review is taking longer because we're defining a novel threat model for LLM-driven execution. This is the right investment — cutting corners on security for agent infrastructure would be irresponsible.' The director was frustrated but understood. The Excel team's onboarding slipped by 3 weeks. I owned the miss in the next sprint review and presented the updated timeline with confidence intervals."

Q: "Why didn't you do a discovery sprint before estimating in the first place?"

A: "Honestly, overconfidence. I'd built containerized services before and assumed sandboxing was 'just containers with more restrictions.' I didn't appreciate that LLM-driven code execution was a fundamentally new threat model that security had never reviewed. The discovery sprint practice came directly from this failure — I literally created the process because this experience showed me I needed it."

Q: "How do you do confidence-level estimates in practice?"

A: "I identify the known work (things I've done before) and the unknown work (novel components, external dependencies). Known work gets a tight range. Unknown work gets a wide range. Then I combine: 'I'm 70% confident we finish in 6 weeks (if unknowns resolve quickly) and 90% confident in 10 weeks (if unknowns are as hard as they could be).' I explicitly list the unknowns so stakeholders can see where the risk is and help de-risk it."

Q: "What would you do if you were in week 3 of a 4-week estimate and realized it would take 10 weeks?"

A: "Communicate immediately — not at the end of week 4. I'd say: 'Here's what I've learned in 3 weeks that changes the estimate. Here are the specific unknowns that expanded. Here's my revised range.' The worst thing you can do is stay silent until the deadline passes. Early transparency preserves trust; late surprises destroy it."

Why This Works at Staff Level

  • Genuine failure: 4 weeks → 10 weeks is a significant miss — not a humble-brag
  • Specific root causes: Security review, cold-start latency, permission boundaries — shows technical depth even in the failure
  • Systemic fix: Discovery sprints and confidence-level estimates — changes the process, not just "I'll try harder"
  • Proved the fix works: Applied the new approach to the next component and delivered on time
My Story: Tax Automation Big-Bang Rollout (Amazon — 2021) ▼

Best for: "Tell me about a time you had to change your approach" or "Describe a failure in how you rolled out a change"

Own it: "I built a technically correct automation pipeline but failed completely on the change management side."

Context: "I was tasked with automating Amazon Business's use tax calculation and reporting pipeline. The existing process was entirely manual — tax analysts spent 3 days per month pulling data from multiple systems, cross-referencing it, and filing reports."

What happened:

  • "I built a fully automated pipeline: data extraction from internal systems, rule-based tax calculations, and automated report generation"
  • "The pipeline was technically sound — it produced correct results validated against historical manual calculations"
  • "I did a big-bang cutover: 'Here's the new automated system, we're turning off the manual process next week'"
  • "The tax team panicked. They didn't trust the system, didn't understand how it made decisions, and couldn't explain results to auditors if questioned"
  • "They ran the manual process in parallel for 3 months — meaning my automation saved zero time and actually increased their workload"

Reflection: "I treated this as a pure engineering problem when it was fundamentally a people and trust problem. The tax team's resistance wasn't irrational — they were personally accountable for tax filings and needed to understand and trust the system before they could let go of manual verification."

Recovery and changes:

  • "Rebuilt the rollout as a shadow-mode approach: automation ran alongside manual for 1 month, showing side-by-side comparisons"
  • "Added an explanation layer: for every calculation, the system showed which rules it applied and why, so analysts could audit the logic"
  • "Trained the tax team on the system in small groups, letting them spot-check results and build confidence gradually"
  • "Created a phased handoff: month 1 shadow mode, month 2 automation-primary with manual spot-checks, month 3 full automation with exception handling"

Application: "I now use trust-building ramps for every automation project. When I later automated parts of the Copilot evaluation pipeline at Microsoft, I started with shadow mode from day one — and adoption happened in half the time."

Anticipated Follow-Up Questions

Q: "Why didn't you involve the tax team earlier in the design?"

A: "That's exactly the failure. I treated it as a pure engineering problem — extract data, apply rules, generate reports. I validated correctness against historical data but never validated usability or trust with the actual users. I assumed that 'correct output = ready to ship.' The tax analysts needed to understand how the system made decisions, not just see that the numbers matched. I should have involved them from week 1, not after the pipeline was built."

Q: "How did the tax team react when you came back with the shadow-mode approach?"

A: "Skeptical at first — I'd already burned trust with the big-bang attempt. The lead analyst said, 'We'll try it, but we're not turning off our process until we're ready.' That was fair. I earned trust back gradually: first by showing side-by-side results that matched perfectly for 3 weeks, then by adding the explanation layer so they could audit the logic. The turning point was when an analyst found a discrepancy in the shadow run — it turned out to be a bug in their manual process, not my automation. After that, confidence shifted."

Q: "How do you apply 'trust-building ramps' now?"

A: "Three principles: (1) Shadow mode first — run the new system alongside the old one and show comparisons before asking anyone to switch. (2) Explain, don't just output — if automation replaces human judgment, show the reasoning, not just the result. (3) Let the users choose when to cut over — don't set the date for them. When I applied this to the Copilot evaluation pipeline at Microsoft, I ran the new pipeline alongside the old one for 2 sprints before anyone depended on it. Adoption happened naturally."

Q: "Is this really a failure? The system worked correctly."

A: "Yes, it's a failure. The system was technically correct but operationally useless for 3 months because the people who needed to use it didn't trust it. Engineering success isn't just 'the code works' — it's 'the users adopt it and it creates value.' By that measure, I failed for 3 months and then spent additional effort on the shadow-mode recovery. Total cost: ~4 months of wasted time and damaged credibility with a key partner team."

Why This Works at Staff Level

  • Empathy for non-engineers: Understood that the tax team's resistance was rational, not obstructionist
  • Process failure, not technical: The code was correct — the rollout strategy was wrong. This is a staff-level insight
  • Transferable learning: "Trust-building ramps" applied successfully in a completely different context at a different company
  • Shows growth: From "deploy and done" to understanding change management as part of the engineering work

Key Language Patterns

Good Phrases

  • "I made a mistake when..."
  • "In hindsight, I should have..."
  • "This taught me..."
  • "I've since changed my approach to..."
  • "When a similar situation arose, I..."

Phrases to Avoid

  • "It wasn't really my fault..."
  • "The team failed to..."
  • "If only they had..."
  • "Looking back, it wasn't that bad..."
  • "I did everything right, but..."

Staff-Level Failure Categories

At staff level, interviewers expect you to have experienced - and learned from - more significant failures. Here are categories to prepare:

Team Failures Where You Were Responsible ▼

As a leader, team failures are your failures. These stories show accountability and organizational awareness.

Types of Team Failures to Prepare

  • Missed deadline due to planning failure: You underestimated scope, dependencies, or team capacity
  • Quality issues that shipped: Testing gaps, code review process failures
  • Morale/burnout problems: You pushed too hard or didn't notice warning signs
  • Communication breakdowns: Stakeholders were surprised, teams were misaligned
  • Hiring mistakes: You advocated for a candidate who didn't work out

Example: Team Delivery Failure

Situation: "I led a team of 5 engineers on a payments compliance project with a regulatory deadline."

What happened: "We missed the deadline by 3 weeks. Two team members burned out and one transferred to another team shortly after."

My role in the failure: "I failed in three ways: I accepted an unrealistic timeline without pushing back with data. I didn't escalate early enough when I saw the team struggling. And I prioritized the deadline over my team's well-being, thinking we could 'push through.'"

What I learned:

  • "Sustainable pace matters more than any single deadline"
  • "My job includes protecting the team from unrealistic commitments"
  • "Early escalation with options is better than late escalation with excuses"

Changes made: "I now build 25% buffer into all estimates. I have weekly 1:1s focused on workload and energy levels. I've learned to say 'We can do X by that date, or Y by this date - which is more important?'"

Key Principles for Team Failure Stories

  • Take responsibility even when others contributed to the failure
  • Never blame team members - frame as systemic or leadership issues
  • Show you understand the broader impact (on people, not just projects)
  • Demonstrate genuine change in how you lead
Technological Failures: Decisions That Didn't Age Well ▼

Some technical decisions seem right at the time but prove problematic months or years later. These stories show long-term thinking development.

Common Patterns

  • Technology choices: Framework/library that was abandoned, technology that didn't scale
  • Build vs. buy: Building something you should have bought (or vice versa)
  • Premature optimization: Over-engineering for scale that never came
  • Under-engineering: Shortcuts that created long-term technical debt
  • Vendor lock-in: Deep integration with a vendor that raised prices or degraded

Example: Framework Choice That Didn't Age Well

Situation: "In 2019, I strongly advocated for using a specific GraphQL framework for our new API layer. It was cutting-edge and seemed perfect for our use case."

What happened: "Two years later, the framework was effectively abandoned by its maintainers. We had 40+ services using it and faced a difficult choice: fork and maintain it ourselves or undergo a costly migration."

My role: "I championed this choice. I was excited about the technology and didn't adequately assess sustainability risk - community size, corporate backing, maintainer activity."

What I learned:

  • "For foundational choices, boring and stable often beats cutting-edge"
  • "Evaluate open-source projects on sustainability, not just features"
  • "Build abstraction layers to reduce switching costs"

Changes made: "I created a technology evaluation rubric that includes: maintainer activity (>12 months), commercial backing, community size, and escape path cost. I now weight stability much higher for core infrastructure decisions."

How to Frame These Stories

  • Acknowledge: "At the time, it seemed like the right choice because..."
  • New information: "What we didn't anticipate was..."
  • Ownership: "I should have considered... / My evaluation missed..."
  • Growth: "My decision framework now includes..."
Architectural & Design Failures ▼

Architecture decisions have long-lasting consequences. Staff engineers should have stories about learning from architectural mistakes.

Types of Architectural Failures

  • Wrong abstraction boundaries: Services that are too coupled or too fragmented
  • Scalability surprises: Design that hit unexpected limits
  • Data model mistakes: Schema decisions that became constraints
  • Integration patterns: Sync vs. async, REST vs. events, etc.
  • Security architecture: Insufficient isolation or access control design

Example: Microservices Granularity Mistake

Situation: "I designed our order management system as 8 microservices, following what I understood to be best practices - one service per domain concept."

What happened: "Within 18 months, we had a distributed monolith. Services were tightly coupled, a single order required 12+ synchronous calls, and our p99 latency was 4 seconds. Debugging was nearly impossible."

My role: "I designed the service boundaries without sufficient understanding of the actual usage patterns. I applied microservices dogmatically rather than pragmatically."

What I learned:

  • "Start with a modular monolith and extract services based on proven boundaries"
  • "Service boundaries should follow team boundaries, not domain concepts"
  • "Distributed systems should have independent deployment and failure domains"
  • "The cost of network calls is easy to underestimate"

Changes made: "We consolidated to 3 services aligned with team ownership. Latency dropped to 200ms. I now advocate for 'monolith first' and require proof of independent scaling needs before service extraction."

Example: Data Model Regret

Situation: "I designed our user preferences system with a flexible JSON column approach - seemed perfect for rapidly evolving requirements."

What happened: "Two years later, we couldn't efficiently query preferences, couldn't enforce data integrity, and had accumulated 50+ undocumented preference keys with inconsistent formats."

My role: "I optimized for developer flexibility over data integrity. I didn't consider the long-term cost of schema-less storage."

What I learned: "Flexibility has a cost. Structured data with clear contracts is almost always worth the upfront investment. 'Schemaless' really means 'schema in every consumer.'"

Changes made: "I now default to explicit schemas with versioning. If we need flexibility, I require a schema registry and migration plan from day one."

Discussing Failures Without Revealing Company Secrets

Confidentiality Guidelines for Failure Stories ▼

What You Can Safely Share

  • General scale: "A system handling millions of requests" (not "our exact revenue numbers")
  • Category of problem: "A database scalability issue" (not "we hit MySQL row limits in the orders table")
  • Your personal learnings: What you would do differently
  • Process improvements: New practices you implemented (at general level)
  • Public information: Anything already in blog posts, conference talks, or news

What You Should NOT Share

  • Specific financial impact: "We lost $X million" unless it's public
  • Customer names or details: "A major customer was affected" not "Company X"
  • Unreleased product information: Future roadmap, cancelled projects
  • Security vulnerability specifics: Attack vectors, unpatched issues
  • Personnel issues: "A team member was fired" - keep focus on your actions
  • Legal or regulatory details: Ongoing litigation, compliance violations

Safe Framing Techniques

Instead of... Say...
"We had a data breach that exposed 100K users" "We had a security incident that required urgent response and led to significant process improvements"
"The CTO made a bad call on..." "The organization chose a direction that I disagreed with, and I learned how to influence decisions earlier"
"We wasted $2M on a project that was cancelled" "A significant initiative was cancelled due to changing priorities. I learned about validating assumptions earlier"
"Our competitor copied our feature and did it better" "Market conditions changed during our development cycle, and we had to pivot"

When in Doubt

Ask yourself: "Would my current employer be comfortable if this appeared in a news article attributed to me?"

If not, generalize or choose a different example. You have plenty of failures to choose from - pick ones you can discuss freely.

Company-Specific Failure Question Patterns

How Major Companies Frame Failure Questions ▼

Different companies emphasize different aspects of failure stories. Tailor your preparation accordingly.

Company What They Emphasize How to Tailor Your Story
Amazon Leadership Principles: "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit," "Deliver Results," "Earn Trust" Emphasize data-driven decision-making, customer impact, and what metrics changed after your correction
Google Intellectual humility, "Googleyness," ability to navigate ambiguity Show how you sought diverse perspectives, remained open to being wrong, and applied rigorous thinking to learn from the failure
Meta "Move fast," learning velocity, building for scale Focus on speed of learning and recovery. How quickly did you recognize and correct the failure? What velocity improvements came from the lesson?
Microsoft Growth mindset (core to culture since Satya Nadella's leadership), inclusivity, customer obsession Explicitly frame the failure as a growth mindset moment. Show how you moved from "I failed" to "I learned" and how you helped others learn too

Important Note

Company values and interview practices evolve. Research the latest interview guides and Glassdoor reports before your specific interview. The patterns above reflect general cultural emphases as of early 2026, but always verify current expectations.

Advanced Scenarios: Growth Through Crisis

Leading Teams Through Failure Recovery ▼

At staff level, you're not just experiencing failures - you're helping teams recover from them. These stories demonstrate leadership maturity.

Recovery Leadership Story Structure

  1. The failure (15%): Brief context - what went wrong
  2. Impact assessment (15%): How you evaluated damage and urgency
  3. Team rally (30%): How you mobilized and supported the team
  4. Recovery execution (25%): Actions taken, adjustments made
  5. Rebuilding (15%): Restoring confidence, implementing improvements

Example: Project Recovery After Launch Failure

Situation: "Our team's much-anticipated feature launch resulted in significant performance issues. Users were complaining, leadership was concerned, and team morale was at rock bottom."

Task: "I needed to stabilize the system, restore user trust, and help the team recover psychologically from a very public failure."

Action:

  • "First, I shielded the team from external pressure - I took all the stakeholder calls myself and gave the team space to focus"
  • "Ran blameless assessment focused on systemic issues, not individual mistakes"
  • "Broke recovery into 24-hour sprints with achievable goals to rebuild momentum"
  • "Celebrated each milestone publicly - 'Database stable,' 'Latency under 200ms,' 'Zero errors this hour'"
  • "After stabilization, facilitated honest retrospective about what went wrong and implemented 5 process changes"

Result: "System stable within 48 hours. More importantly, the team went on to ship three successful features that quarter with the improved process. Two team members later told me that experience shaped how they lead through crises."

Multi-Year Growth Arc Stories ▼

The most compelling growth stories span years and show fundamental evolution in how you think and work.

Multi-Year Arc Structure

  1. Starting point: How you used to approach things
  2. Pattern of failures: Multiple related mistakes (not just one)
  3. Moment of realization: When you understood the underlying pattern
  4. Fundamental change: How your approach shifted
  5. Evidence of growth: Different outcomes with new approach

Example: Evolution as a Technical Leader

Starting point: "Early in my career, I believed the best technical solution should always win. I'd write detailed analysis, present data, and expect rational decision-making."

Pattern of failures: "Multiple times, technically superior solutions were rejected. I blamed politics, said people weren't 'getting it.' I was frustrated and felt undervalued."

Realization: "After my third major proposal was rejected, my mentor pointed out that I was always presenting solutions to my problems, not stakeholders' problems. I was speaking engineer, not business."

Fundamental change: "I learned that technical decisions are business decisions. Every proposal now starts with 'What problem does this solve for whom?' and 'What does success look like in business terms?' The technical analysis comes after establishing shared context."

Evidence: "My proposal acceptance rate went from ~30% to ~80%. More importantly, even rejected proposals led to productive conversations about priorities. I stopped feeling like I was fighting the organization."

Strong Multi-Year Arc Topics

  • From individual contributor mindset to leader mindset
  • From perfectionism to pragmatic delivery
  • From conflict avoidance to productive disagreement
  • From hero complex to team multiplication
  • From technical focus to business awareness
